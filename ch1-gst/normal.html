📦 GST — Deep Learning

1. The Story (Finland Phenomenon)​

🎮 Arjun's Gaming Rig

Arjun walks into a Delhi electronics shop to buy a graphics card. The sticker says ₹40,000 (Marked Price). The shopkeeper offers a 10% festival discount. The government charges 18% GST on electronics. Both seller and buyer are in Delhi — intra-state transaction.



How much does Arjun actually pay? Where does the tax money go? Why is the tax not on ₹40,000? Every formula in this chapter answers one of those three questions.

Before GST (pre-2017), India had VAT, Excise Duty, Service Tax, Entry Tax — each charged differently. A single product could be taxed 5–6 times from factory to consumer. GST replaced all of these with one unified tax.

TypeWhat it means
Direct TaxPaid directly by the earner — e.g. Income Tax
Indirect Tax (GST)Collected from the consumer through the seller. The seller is the government's tax collector.

2. Core Vocabulary​

TermMeaningArjun's GPU
Marked Price (M.P.)Price tagged before discount₹40,000
Discountd% × M.P. ÷ 100₹4,000 (10%)
Selling Price (S.P.)After discount, before GST₹36,000
Taxable Value= S.P. — GST is applied here₹36,000 ⚠️

3. Bar Model — Singapore CPA Approach​

Singapore Math never introduces a formula before a student sees it concretely and pictorially first.

  • 🧱 Concrete
  • 🖼️ Pictorial (Bar Model)
  • 🔢 Abstract (Formulas)

Stage 1 — Concrete: What physically happens to the money.

Arjun hands over ₹42,480. The shopkeeper keeps ₹36,000 (his selling price). He sends ₹3,240 (CGST) to the Central Government and ₹3,240 (SGST) to the Delhi State Government. The ₹4,000 discount was a gift from the shopkeeper — the government never touches it.

Who gets itAmount
🏪 Shopkeeper₹36,000
🏛️ Central Govt (CGST)₹3,240
🏛️ State Govt (SGST)₹3,240
🎁 Discount (vanishes)₹4,000 — no GST on this

Stage 2 — Pictorial: See the money flow as proportional bars.

Why the bar model matters: The discount piece disappears before the GST bars appear. This makes it visually impossible to accidentally calculate GST on the Marked Price.

Stage 3 — Abstract: Only now do we meet symbols — they describe what you already saw.

SP = MP × (100 − d) / 100

Arjun: 40,000 × 90/100 = ₹36,000

GST = r% × SP ÷ 100

Arjun: 18% × 36,000 = ₹6,480

Bill = SP × (100 + r) / 100

Arjun: 36,000 × 118/100 = ₹42,480

Bill = MP × (100−d)/100 × (100+r)/100

One-step master formula. Combine discount and GST in a single calculation.

⚠️ The #1 Exam Trap

GST is on S.P. (after discount), never on M.P.
Wrong: 18% × ₹40,000 = ₹7,200  |  Correct: 18% × ₹36,000 = ₹6,480

4. CGST vs SGST vs IGST​

  • 🏙️ Intra-State (Same State)
  • 🗺️ Inter-State (Different States)

Rule: Intra-state → CGST + SGST. Each = Total GST Rate ÷ 2.
At 18%: CGST = 9%, SGST = 9%.

Rule: Inter-state → only IGST. IGST Rate = Total GST Rate.

⚠️ Never Mix Them

A transaction can't have both IGST and CGST/SGST. It's one or the other.

5. Formulas in 4 Languages (Finland Languaging)​

Finland's research: a student owns a formula only when they can express it in 4 ways.

Formula 1 — Standard Bill​

  • 🗣️ Words
  • 👁️ Visual
  • 🔣 Math
  • ✋ Try It

Subtract the discount from the Marked Price to get the Selling Price (Taxable Value). Then add a percentage of only that amount as GST. The customer pays SP + GST.

[MP] → chop off discount → [SP] → glue on GST → [BILL]

(See the bar model in Section 3)

SP = MP × (100−d)/100
Bill = SP × (100+r)/100
Bill = MP × (100−d)/100 × (100+r)/100

MP = ₹5,000, Discount = 20%, GST = 12%. Find the bill.

  • SP = 5,000 × 80/100 = ₹4,000
  • Bill = 4,000 × 112/100 = ₹4,480

Formula 2 — Reverse (GST-Inclusive Price)​

  • 🗣️ Words
  • 👁️ Visual
  • 🔣 Math
  • ✋ Try It

When the given price already includes GST, it equals (100 + r)% of the original price. To strip the GST out, divide by (100 + r) and multiply by 100.

[Price A = 118% of SP] → divide by 118 → multiply by 100 → [SP]

Think: ₹118 includes ₹18 GST. So ₹100 out of every ₹118 is the actual price.

SP = A × 100 / (100 + r)
GST in A = A × r / (100 + r)

where A = the given inclusive price, r = GST rate %

Customer pays ₹2,360 including 18% GST. Find the original price and GST.

  • SP = 2,360 × 100/118 = ₹2,000
  • GST = 2,360 − 2,000 = ₹360

Formula 3 — Net GST Payable (for Dealers)​

  • 🗣️ Words
  • 👁️ Visual
  • 🔣 Math
  • ✋ Try It

A dealer collects GST from customers (output tax) but also paid GST when buying stock (input tax). The government only wants the difference — the tax on the dealer's value added.

OUTPUT TAX (collected from buyer) − INPUT TAX CREDIT (paid to supplier) = NET GST PAYABLE

Net GST = Output Tax − Input Tax Credit
Net GST = (r/100) × (SP − CP) [same rate only]

Dealer buys at ₹20,000, sells at ₹28,000. GST = 12%. Find net GST.

  • Output Tax = 12% × 28,000 = ₹3,360
  • Input Tax = 12% × 20,000 = ₹2,400
  • Net GST = 3,360 − 2,400 = ₹960
  • Shortcut: 12% × (28,000−20,000) = 12% × 8,000 = ₹960 ✓

6. Supply Chain & Input Tax Credit​

Key insight: The government collects the same total tax (₹6,480) regardless of how many stages there are. Each dealer forwards only their piece — the tax on what they added.

⚠️ Consumer Cannot Claim ITC

Only registered GST dealers can claim Input Tax Credit. The final consumer (Arjun) pays the full GST and cannot get it back.

7. Worked Examples​

  • 📗 Basic
  • 📙 Reverse
  • 📘 ITC Chain
  • 📕 Rate Change

Q1 — Discount + CGST + SGST

A television is marked at ₹45,000. A 15% discount is given. GST = 28% (intra-state). Find (i) S.P. (ii) CGST (iii) SGST (iv) Amount paid by buyer.

  1. SP = 45,000 × 85/100 = ₹38,250
  2. CGST Rate = SGST Rate = 28/2 = 14% each
  3. CGST = SGST = 14/100 × 38,250 = ₹5,355 each
  4. Bill = 38,250 + 5,355 + 5,355 = ₹48,960
✓ SP = ₹38,250 | CGST = ₹5,355 | SGST = ₹5,355 | Bill = ₹48,960

Q2 — Successive Discounts + GST

MP = ₹10,000. Discounts of 10% then 5%. GST = 12%. Find bill.

  1. After 1st discount: 10,000 × 90/100 = ₹9,000
  2. After 2nd discount: 9,000 × 95/100 = ₹8,550 ← taxable value
  3. Bill = 8,550 × 112/100 = ₹9,576
⚠️ Why not 15%?

10% + 5% = 14.5% equivalent, not 15%. Apply one by one.

Q3 — Price Inclusive of GST

Priya pays ₹7,080 for a saree that includes 18% GST. Find original price and GST.

  1. ₹7,080 = 118% of SP → SP = 7,080 × 100/118 = ₹6,000
  2. GST = 7,080 − 6,000 = ₹1,080
✓ Price before GST = ₹6,000 | GST = ₹1,080

Q4 — Find Marked Price from Bill

Customer pays ₹28,320 after 20% discount and 18% GST. Find Marked Price.

  1. Let MP = x. SP = x × 80/100
  2. Bill = SP × 118/100 → x × 80/100 × 118/100 = 28,320
  3. x = 28,320 × 10000/9440 = ₹30,000
✓ Marked Price = ₹30,000

Q5 — Net GST Payable

Wholesaler buys at ₹50,000, sells at ₹65,000. GST = 12% (intra-state). Find: (i) Input Tax (ii) Output Tax (iii) Net GST (iv) Invoice to retailer.

  1. Input Tax = 12/100 × 50,000 = ₹6,000
  2. Output Tax = 12/100 × 65,000 = ₹7,800
  3. Net GST = 7,800 − 6,000 = ₹1,800 (Shortcut: 12% × 15,000 = ₹1,800 ✓)
  4. Invoice = 65,000 + 7,800 = ₹72,800
✓ Input Tax = ₹6,000 | Output Tax = ₹7,800 | Net GST = ₹1,800 | Invoice = ₹72,800

Q6 — GST Rate Reduction

Pre-tax price of an AC is ₹30,000. GST drops from 28% to 18%. By how much does the consumer's price change?

  1. Old Bill = 30,000 × 128/100 = ₹38,400
  2. New Bill = 30,000 × 118/100 = ₹35,400
  3. Reduction = 38,400 − 35,400 = ₹3,000
  4. Shortcut: 30,000 × (28−18)/100 = ₹3,000 ✓
✓ Consumer price reduced by ₹3,000
