3. Scale Factor — The Zoom Number

The one idea that drives everything​

When two triangles are similar, there is one number — the scale factor k — that connects them completely.

Scale factor k = (any side of the new triangle) ÷ (corresponding side of the original triangle)

It doesn't matter which pair of sides you use — you'll always get the same answer k.

Visualising the scale factor​

In the diagram:

  • The blue triangle (solid lines) is the original — △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC
  • The green triangle (dashed lines) is the enlarged version — △A′B′C′\triangle A'B'C'△A′B′C′
  • Every point moves away from O by a factor of k = 2
  • Every side gets twice as long
  • The angles stay completely unchanged

Three types of scale factor​

  • k > 1 (Enlargement)
  • k < 1 (Reduction)
  • k = 1 (Congruence)

When k>1k > 1k>1, the new triangle is bigger than the original.

Example: △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC has sides 3, 4, 5. k=3k = 3k=3.

New triangle sides: 3×3=93 \times 3 = 93×3=9, 3×4=123 \times 4 = 123×4=12, 3×5=153 \times 5 = 153×5=15.

93=124=155=3=k✓\dfrac{9}{3} = \dfrac{12}{4} = \dfrac{15}{5} = 3 = k \checkmark39​=412​=515​=3=k✓

Real life: Zooming in on a map. Making a model bigger than the original design.

When k<1k < 1k<1, the new triangle is smaller than the original.

Example: △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC has sides 10, 8, 6. k=0.5k = 0.5k=0.5.

New triangle sides: 0.5×10=50.5 \times 10 = 50.5×10=5, 0.5×8=40.5 \times 8 = 40.5×8=4, 0.5×6=30.5 \times 6 = 30.5×6=3.

510=48=36=0.5=k✓\dfrac{5}{10} = \dfrac{4}{8} = \dfrac{3}{6} = 0.5 = k \checkmark105​=84​=63​=0.5=k✓

Real life: A map where 1 cm = 2 km. Making a model of a building (much smaller than reality).

When k=1k = 1k=1, the new shape is identical to the original — they are congruent.

Example: △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC has sides 5, 7, 9. k=1k = 1k=1.

New triangle sides: 1×5=51 \times 5 = 51×5=5, 1×7=71 \times 7 = 71×7=7, 1×9=91 \times 9 = 91×9=9.

Same triangle. Congruent.

This proves: Every congruent figure is similar with k=1k = 1k=1.

How to find k (the most important skill)​

Rule

To find the scale factor, divide any corresponding side of the new shape by the original shape.

k=new sideoriginal sidek = \dfrac{\text{new side}}{\text{original side}}k=original sidenew side​

Important: Always divide new ÷ original, not original ÷ new.

But wait — how do I know which sides "correspond"?

The corresponding sides are the ones opposite equal angles. In △ABC∼△PQR\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR△ABC∼△PQR:

  • Side BCBCBC (opposite ∠A\angle A∠A) corresponds to side QRQRQR (opposite ∠P\angle P∠P, since ∠A=∠P\angle A = \angle P∠A=∠P)
  • Side CACACA (opposite ∠B\angle B∠B) corresponds to side RPRPRP (opposite ∠Q\angle Q∠Q)
  • Side ABABAB (opposite ∠C\angle C∠C) corresponds to side PQPQPQ (opposite ∠R\angle R∠R)

Finding unknown sides using k​

This is the main calculation skill for ICSE similarity questions.

Method:

  1. Identify that two triangles are similar and write the correspondence
  2. Find k using a pair of sides you know completely
  3. Use k to find the unknown side
  • Example 1 — Basic
  • Example 2 — Find Original
  • Example 3 — Mixed

△ABC∼△PQR\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR△ABC∼△PQR. AB=4AB = 4AB=4, PQ=10PQ = 10PQ=10, BC=6BC = 6BC=6. Find QRQRQR.

Step 1 — Find k: k=PQAB=104=2.5k = \dfrac{PQ}{AB} = \dfrac{10}{4} = 2.5k=ABPQ​=410​=2.5

Step 2 — Find QR: QRBC=k⇒QR=k×BC=2.5×6=15\dfrac{QR}{BC} = k \Rightarrow QR = k \times BC = 2.5 \times 6 = 15BCQR​=k⇒QR=k×BC=2.5×6=15

△ABC∼△PQR\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR△ABC∼△PQR with k=3k = 3k=3. QR=18QR = 18QR=18. Find BCBCBC.

Here we know the new side and need the original.

Since k=QRBCk = \dfrac{QR}{BC}k=BCQR​: BC=QRk=183=6BC = \dfrac{QR}{k} = \dfrac{18}{3} = 6BC=kQR​=318​=6

In △DEF∼△XYZ\triangle DEF \sim \triangle XYZ△DEF∼△XYZ: DE=6DE = 6DE=6, EF=8EF = 8EF=8, XY=9XY = 9XY=9. Find YZYZYZ and the scale factor.

Step 1 — Find k: k=XYDE=96=1.5k = \dfrac{XY}{DE} = \dfrac{9}{6} = 1.5k=DEXY​=69​=1.5

Step 2 — Find YZ (corresponds to EF): YZ=k×EF=1.5×8=12YZ = k \times EF = 1.5 \times 8 = 12YZ=k×EF=1.5×8=12

Check: YZEF=128=1.5=k\dfrac{YZ}{EF} = \dfrac{12}{8} = 1.5 = kEFYZ​=812​=1.5=k ✓

The proportionality equation​

When triangles are similar, the corresponding sides form a proportion. You can write it as:

ABPQ=BCQR=CARP\dfrac{AB}{PQ} = \dfrac{BC}{QR} = \dfrac{CA}{RP}PQAB​=QRBC​=RPCA​

This is one equation in disguise — all three fractions equal k.

Cross-multiplying gives you ways to find unknowns:

AB×QR=PQ×BC(from the first two fractions)AB \times QR = PQ \times BC \quad \text{(from the first two fractions)}AB×QR=PQ×BC(from the first two fractions)

Do not mix up the order

Always write: side from △1side from △2=side from △1side from △2\dfrac{\text{side from } \triangle 1}{\text{side from } \triangle 2} = \dfrac{\text{side from } \triangle 1}{\text{side from } \triangle 2}side from △2side from △1​=side from △2side from △1​

Never mix: ABQR\dfrac{AB}{QR}QRAB​ — this puts sides from both triangles in the same fraction. That's wrong.

What scale factor does NOT tell you​

The scale factor kkk tells you about lengths. But:

  • Angles are not multiplied by k — they stay the same
  • Area is not multiplied by k — it's multiplied by k² (we'll cover this later)
  • Perimeter IS multiplied by k — since all sides scale by k
