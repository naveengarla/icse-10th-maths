6. Basic Proportionality Theorem (BPT)

One theorem, two names​

This theorem is known by two names:

  • Basic Proportionality Theorem (BPT) — the official ICSE name
  • Thales' Theorem — named after the ancient Greek mathematician

Both refer to exactly the same result.

The theorem — what it says​

Statement of BPT

If a line is drawn parallel to one side of a triangle, it divides the other two sides in the same ratio.

In △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC, if DE∥BCDE \parallel BCDE∥BC (where DDD is on ABABAB and EEE is on ACACAC), then:

ADDB=AEEC\dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}DBAD​=ECAE​

See it visually:

Why does this work? The intuition​

Imagine stretching a rubber band from vertex AAA down to BCBCBC. Any horizontal slice through the band cuts it in the same proportion — because you're scaling everything equally from point AAA.

More formally: DE∥BCDE \parallel BCDE∥BC means △ADE∼△ABC\triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC△ADE∼△ABC (by AA). The similarity gives proportional sides, which gives the ratio result.

The two forms — don't confuse them​

Form 1 (the BPT ratio): ADDB=AEEC\dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}DBAD​=ECAE​ Ratio of the two parts of each side

Form 2 (from similarity): ADAB=AEAC=DEBC\dfrac{AD}{AB} = \dfrac{AE}{AC} = \dfrac{DE}{BC}ABAD​=ACAE​=BCDE​ Ratio of part to the whole side

Both are useful. Form 2 lets you find DEDEDE.

The most common error

Students mix up Form 1 and Form 2.

ADDB\dfrac{AD}{DB}DBAD​ is NOT the same as ADAB\dfrac{AD}{AB}ABAD​.

If AD=3AD = 3AD=3 and DB=5DB = 5DB=5:

  • ADDB=35\dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{3}{5}DBAD​=53​ (Form 1)
  • ADAB=38\dfrac{AD}{AB} = \dfrac{3}{8}ABAD​=83​ (Form 2 — using the whole side AB=3+5=8AB = 3+5 = 8AB=3+5=8)

Converse of BPT​

The converse is also true and equally important:

Converse of BPT

If a line divides two sides of a triangle in the same ratio, then that line is parallel to the third side.

If ADDB=AEEC\dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}DBAD​=ECAE​, then DE∥BCDE \parallel BCDE∥BC.

Use the converse when you need to prove a line is parallel.

Worked examples​

  • Find a length
  • Find DE
  • Use the Converse
  • Midpoint case

Q: In △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC, DE∥BCDE \parallel BCDE∥BC. AD=4AD = 4AD=4, DB=6DB = 6DB=6, AE=5AE = 5AE=5. Find ECECEC.

Solution:

By BPT: ADDB=AEEC\dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}DBAD​=ECAE​

46=5EC\dfrac{4}{6} = \dfrac{5}{EC}64​=EC5​

EC=5×64=304=7.5EC = \dfrac{5 \times 6}{4} = \dfrac{30}{4} = 7.5EC=45×6​=430​=7.5

Q: In △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC, DE∥BCDE \parallel BCDE∥BC. AD=3AD = 3AD=3, AB=9AB = 9AB=9, BC=12BC = 12BC=12. Find DEDEDE.

Solution:

Using Form 2 (part to whole): ADAB=DEBC\dfrac{AD}{AB} = \dfrac{DE}{BC}ABAD​=BCDE​

39=DE12\dfrac{3}{9} = \dfrac{DE}{12}93​=12DE​

DE=3×129=4DE = \dfrac{3 \times 12}{9} = 4DE=93×12​=4

Q: In △PQR\triangle PQR△PQR, DDD is on PQPQPQ and EEE is on PRPRPR with PD=4PD = 4PD=4, DQ=8DQ = 8DQ=8, PE=3PE = 3PE=3, ER=6ER = 6ER=6. Is DE∥QRDE \parallel QRDE∥QR?

Solution:

Check: PDDQ=48=12\dfrac{PD}{DQ} = \dfrac{4}{8} = \dfrac{1}{2}DQPD​=84​=21​ and PEER=36=12\dfrac{PE}{ER} = \dfrac{3}{6} = \dfrac{1}{2}ERPE​=63​=21​

Both ratios equal 12\dfrac{1}{2}21​. By the converse of BPT, DE∥QRDE \parallel QRDE∥QR. ✓

Q: DDD and EEE are midpoints of ABABAB and ACACAC in △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC. What is DEDEDE in terms of BCBCBC?

Solution:

DDD is midpoint → AD=DBAD = DBAD=DB → ADDB=1\dfrac{AD}{DB} = 1DBAD​=1

By BPT → AEEC=1\dfrac{AE}{EC} = 1ECAE​=1 → EEE is midpoint of ACACAC ✓ (consistent)

Using Form 2: ADAB=12\dfrac{AD}{AB} = \dfrac{1}{2}ABAD​=21​ → DEBC=12\dfrac{DE}{BC} = \dfrac{1}{2}BCDE​=21​ → DE=BC2DE = \dfrac{BC}{2}DE=2BC​

This is the Mid-Point Theorem: The line joining midpoints of two sides = half the third side and is parallel to it.

Exam writing format​

In △ABC, DE ∥ BC (given)
By BPT: AD/DB = AE/EC
        4/6   = 5/EC
∴ EC = 30/4 = 7.5

Always state "By BPT" explicitly — that earns a method mark.

Summary​

BPT at a glance

Statement: DE∥BCDE \parallel BCDE∥BC → ADDB=AEEC\dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}DBAD​=ECAE​

Also gives: ADAB=AEAC=DEBC\dfrac{AD}{AB} = \dfrac{AE}{AC} = \dfrac{DE}{BC}ABAD​=ACAE​=BCDE​ (from the similarity △ADE∼△ABC\triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC△ADE∼△ABC)

Converse: ADDB=AEEC\dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}DBAD​=ECAE​ → DE∥BCDE \parallel BCDE∥BC

Special case: DDD, EEE are midpoints → DE=12BCDE = \dfrac{1}{2}BCDE=21​BC (Mid-Point Theorem)
