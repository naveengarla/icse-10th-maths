4. The Three Conditions — AA, SAS, SSS

Why do we need conditions at all?​

In the previous pages you learned that similar triangles have equal angles and proportional sides. But to check ALL of that for two triangles, you'd need to measure 3 angles and 3 sides — 6 measurements every time.

Mathematicians discovered shortcuts. In some cases, checking just 2 angles or 1 angle + 2 sides is enough to guarantee similarity. These shortcuts are the three conditions.

The three conditions — overview
ConditionWhat to checkHow much work
AA2 pairs of equal anglesEasiest — most common
SAS1 equal angle + 2 proportional sides AROUND itMedium
SSSAll 3 pairs of sides proportionalMost work

In 80% of ICSE questions: use AA.

Condition 1 — AA (Angle-Angle)​

Start here: why 2 angles are enough​

Before we look at any triangles, let's understand ONE fact that makes AA possible:

In any triangle, all three angles add up to 180°. So if you know TWO angles, the third is completely determined — it has no choice.

The implication: If ∠A=∠P\angle A = \angle P∠A=∠P and ∠B=∠Q\angle B = \angle Q∠B=∠Q in two triangles, then mathematically:

∠C=180°−∠A−∠B=180°−∠P−∠Q=∠R\angle C = 180° - \angle A - \angle B = 180° - \angle P - \angle Q = \angle R∠C=180°−∠A−∠B=180°−∠P−∠Q=∠R

The third angle MUST match too. So checking just 2 is genuinely sufficient.

The AA condition stated precisely​

AA Condition

If two angles of one triangle are equal to two angles of another triangle, the triangles are similar.

If ∠A=∠P\angle A = \angle P∠A=∠P and ∠B=∠Q\angle B = \angle Q∠B=∠Q, then △ABC∼△PQR\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR△ABC∼△PQR.

Where do equal angles come from? (The 3 sources)​

This is the part students often miss. In an exam figure, equal angles don't appear by magic — they come from one of three geometric facts. You need to recognise these on sight.

  • Source 1 — Common Angle
  • Source 2 — Parallel Lines
  • Source 3 — Vertically Opposite

A shared vertex gives a common angle.

When two triangles share a vertex point, the angle at that vertex is the same angle in both triangles.

Example: △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC and △ADE\triangle ADE△ADE both have vertex AAA at the top.

∠DAE=∠BAC\angle DAE = \angle BAC∠DAE=∠BAC — they are literally the SAME angle.

✅ Write in proof: "∠A\angle A∠A is common to both triangles"

Parallel lines create equal corresponding angles.

When a line DEDEDE is parallel to BCBCBC, the transversal ABABAB cuts both lines creating corresponding angles.

∠ADE=∠ABC\angle ADE = \angle ABC∠ADE=∠ABC (corresponding angles, DE∥BCDE \parallel BCDE∥BC)

Similarly: ∠AED=∠ACB\angle AED = \angle ACB∠AED=∠ACB

✅ Write in proof: "∠ADE=∠ABC\angle ADE = \angle ABC∠ADE=∠ABC (corresponding angles, DE∥BCDE \parallel BCDE∥BC)"

Two lines crossing create equal vertically opposite angles.

When diagonals of a quadrilateral cross at point OOO:

∠AOB=∠COD\angle AOB = \angle COD∠AOB=∠COD (vertically opposite angles)

This is very common in trapezium problems where diagonals intersect.

✅ Write in proof: "∠AOB=∠COD\angle AOB = \angle COD∠AOB=∠COD (vertically opposite angles)"

AA — Step by step example (the most common ICSE type)​

Exam format — write it exactly like this
In △ADE and △ABC:
  ∠DAE = ∠BAC    ... (common angle at A)
  ∠ADE = ∠ABC    ... (corresponding angles, DE ∥ BC)
∴ △ADE ~ △ABC    ... (AA)

Two reasons, two angles, one conclusion. That's 3 marks in ICSE.

AA — More examples with increasing difficulty​

  • Example 1 — Basic
  • Example 2 — Shared Angle
  • Example 3 — Diagonals
  • Example 4 — Right Angles

Figure: △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC with ∠A=65°\angle A = 65°∠A=65°, ∠B=50°\angle B = 50°∠B=50°. Another △PQR\triangle PQR△PQR with ∠P=65°\angle P = 65°∠P=65°, ∠Q=50°\angle Q = 50°∠Q=50°.

Proof:

In △ABC and △PQR:
  ∠A = ∠P = 65°   ... (given)
  ∠B = ∠Q = 50°   ... (given)
∴ △ABC ~ △PQR     ... (AA)

Note: We didn't need to check ∠C\angle C∠C and ∠R\angle R∠R — they're automatically both 180°−65°−50°=65°180° - 65° - 50° = 65°180°−65°−50°=65°.

Figure: In △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC, DDD is on ABABAB and EEE is on ACACAC such that DE∥BCDE \parallel BCDE∥BC.

Prove △ADE∼△ABC\triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC△ADE∼△ABC.

In △ADE and △ABC:
  ∠DAE = ∠BAC   ... (same angle — A is common vertex)
  ∠ADE = ∠ABC   ... (DE ∥ BC → corresponding angles)
∴ △ADE ~ △ABC   ... (AA)

Careful with letter order: A matches A (common), D matches B (both have angle β), E matches C. So the correct statement is △ADE∼△ABC\triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC△ADE∼△ABC — NOT △ADE∼△BAC\triangle ADE \sim \triangle BAC△ADE∼△BAC.

Figure: ABCDABCDABCD is a trapezium with AB∥CDAB \parallel CDAB∥CD. Diagonals meet at OOO. Prove △AOB∼△COD\triangle AOB \sim \triangle COD△AOB∼△COD.

In △AOB and △COD:
  ∠AOB = ∠COD   ... (vertically opposite angles)
  ∠OAB = ∠OCD   ... (AB ∥ CD → alternate angles)
∴ △AOB ~ △COD   ... (AA)

Figure: In △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC, ∠BAC=90°\angle BAC = 90°∠BAC=90°. AD⊥BCAD \perp BCAD⊥BC where DDD is on BCBCBC. Prove △ABD∼△CAD\triangle ABD \sim \triangle CAD△ABD∼△CAD.

Trick: Both triangles have a 90° angle. Find ONE more matching angle.

In △ABD and △CAD:
  ∠ADB = ∠CDA = 90°   ... (AD ⊥ BC, given)
  ∠ABD = ∠CAD         ... (both = 90° − ∠BAD → complementary)
∴ △ABD ~ △CAD        ... (AA)

Why ∠ABD=∠CAD\angle ABD = \angle CAD∠ABD=∠CAD? In △ABD\triangle ABD△ABD: ∠ABD+∠BAD=90°\angle ABD + \angle BAD = 90°∠ABD+∠BAD=90°. In △CAD\triangle CAD△CAD: ∠CAD+∠ACD=90°\angle CAD + \angle ACD = 90°∠CAD+∠ACD=90°. But ∠ACD=∠ABD\angle ACD = \angle ABD∠ACD=∠ABD (same angle from the original). So ∠BAD=∠ACD\angle BAD = \angle ACD∠BAD=∠ACD... work through it carefully.

AA — Edge cases and traps​

  • ❌ Only 1 angle equal
  • ❌ Wrong letter order
  • ✅ When only 1 angle is given

Wrong: "∠A = ∠P (given). Therefore △ABC ~ △PQR by AA."

Why wrong: AA requires two pairs of equal angles. One is not enough — you could have the same angle but completely different other angles.

Fix: Find the second pair before concluding.

Wrong: ∠A=∠Q\angle A = \angle Q∠A=∠Q and ∠B=∠P\angle B = \angle P∠B=∠P. Writing "△ABC∼△PQR\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR△ABC∼△PQR."

Why wrong: If A matches Q and B matches P, then the correct statement is △ABC∼△QPR\triangle ABC \sim \triangle QPR△ABC∼△QPR (Q first, P second, R third).

Rule: The position of the letter in the similarity statement MUST match the vertex with the equal angle.

Situation: You know ∠A=∠P\angle A = \angle P∠A=∠P but no other angles are given.

What to do: Look for a reason why another pair might be equal:

  1. Is there a common angle?
  2. Are there parallel lines creating corresponding angles?
  3. Are there right angles given?

If you find none, AA doesn't apply — try SAS or SSS.

Condition 2 — SAS (Side-Angle-Side)​

The word "included" explained carefully​

SAS sounds simple but has one word that confuses everyone: included.

The equal angle must be included between the two proportional sides. This means: the angle must be at the vertex where those two sides meet.

Think of it this way: If you pick any vertex of a triangle, that vertex has exactly two sides coming out of it. The angle at that vertex is "included" by those two sides — it sits between them.

SAS stated precisely​

SAS Condition

If one angle of a triangle equals one angle of another triangle, AND the two sides FORMING that angle are proportional, the triangles are similar.

If ∠B=∠Q\angle B = \angle Q∠B=∠Q AND ABPQ=BCQR\dfrac{AB}{PQ} = \dfrac{BC}{QR}PQAB​=QRBC​, then △ABC∼△PQR\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR△ABC∼△PQR.

How to apply SAS — step by step​

Step 1: Identify the equal angles (which vertices match).

Step 2: Check the two sides AT each of those vertices (the included sides).

Step 3: Compute their ratios. Are they equal?

Step 4: If yes → SAS. Write the conclusion.

  • Example 1 — Basic SAS
  • Example 2 — Find Unknown
  • ❌ SAS Trap — Wrong Angle

Given: △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC and △PQR\triangle PQR△PQR with ∠B=∠Q=70°\angle B = \angle Q = 70°∠B=∠Q=70°, AB=4AB = 4AB=4, PQ=6PQ = 6PQ=6, BC=6BC = 6BC=6, QR=9QR = 9QR=9.

Step 1: Equal angle at B and Q. So B↔Q in the similarity.

Step 2: Sides at B: ABABAB and BCBCBC. Sides at Q: PQPQPQ and QRQRQR.

Step 3: PQAB=64=1.5\dfrac{PQ}{AB} = \dfrac{6}{4} = 1.5ABPQ​=46​=1.5 and QRBC=96=1.5\dfrac{QR}{BC} = \dfrac{9}{6} = 1.5BCQR​=69​=1.5. Equal ✓

Proof:

In △ABC and △PQR:
  ∠B = ∠Q = 70°         ... (given)
  AB/PQ = BC/QR = 3/2   ... (given: 4/6 = 6/9 = 2/3)
∴ △ABC ~ △PQR           ... (SAS)

Given: △DEF∼△XYZ\triangle DEF \sim \triangle XYZ△DEF∼△XYZ by SAS. ∠E=∠Y\angle E = \angle Y∠E=∠Y, DE=5DE = 5DE=5, XY=10XY = 10XY=10, EF=8EF = 8EF=8. Find YZYZYZ.

Since ∠E=∠Y\angle E = \angle Y∠E=∠Y and SAS applies:

XYDE=YZEF\dfrac{XY}{DE} = \dfrac{YZ}{EF}DEXY​=EFYZ​

105=YZ8\dfrac{10}{5} = \dfrac{YZ}{8}510​=8YZ​

YZ=2×8=16YZ = 2 \times 8 = 16YZ=2×8=16

Wrong attempt: ∠A=∠P\angle A = \angle P∠A=∠P, AB=3AB = 3AB=3, PQ=6PQ = 6PQ=6, BC=4BC = 4BC=4, QR=8QR = 8QR=8. "SAS applies."

Why wrong: ∠A\angle A∠A is at vertex A, which is between sides ABABAB and ACACAC. But BCBCBC is the side opposite to AAA — NOT adjacent to AAA.

For SAS at ∠A\angle A∠A: you need sides ABABAB and ACACAC to be proportional (not ABABAB and BCBCBC).

Moral: The two proportional sides MUST both come out of the equal angle's vertex.

Condition 3 — SSS (Side-Side-Side)​

The idea​

If ALL THREE pairs of corresponding sides are in the same ratio, the triangles must be similar.

SSS Condition

If ABPQ=BCQR=CARP\dfrac{AB}{PQ} = \dfrac{BC}{QR} = \dfrac{CA}{RP}PQAB​=QRBC​=RPCA​, then △ABC∼△PQR\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR△ABC∼△PQR.

How to apply SSS — step by step​

The challenge with SSS: You need to know which sides correspond. Unlike AA, where angles tell you the matching, SSS requires you to figure out the matching from the side ratios themselves.

Method: Sort all sides from smallest to largest in each triangle. Match smallest↔smallest, middle↔middle, largest↔largest. Then check all three ratios.

  • Example 1 — Basic
  • Example 2 — NOT similar
  • ❌ SSS Trap — Wrong pairing

△ABC\triangle ABC△ABC: sides 3, 4, 5. △PQR\triangle PQR△PQR: sides 6, 8, 10.

Sort and match:

△ABC△PQRRatio
3 (BC, smallest)6 (QR, smallest)6/3 = 2
4 (AB, middle)8 (PQ, middle)8/4 = 2
5 (CA, largest)10 (RP, largest)10/5 = 2

All ratios = 2. ∴△ABC∼△PQR\therefore \triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR∴△ABC∼△PQR (SSS) with k=2k = 2k=2.

△ABC\triangle ABC△ABC: sides 4, 6, 8. △PQR\triangle PQR△PQR: sides 6, 9, 13.

△ABC△PQRRatio
466/4 = 1.5
699/6 = 1.5
81313/8 = 1.625

Third ratio differs. Not similar. Even one unequal ratio breaks SSS.

Wrong: △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC has sides 3, 5, 7. △PQR\triangle PQR△PQR has sides 6, 10, 14.

Student checks: 6/3=26/3=26/3=2, 14/5=2.814/5=2.814/5=2.8 ← ❌

Why wrong: They matched 14 (largest of PQR) with 5 (middle of ABC). Always pair same rank sides.

Correct: 6/3=2, 10/5=2, 14/7=2. Similar! ✓

Which condition? What is the similarity statement?

∠M=∠Y=50°\angle M = \angle Y = 50°∠M=∠Y=50° (equal included angle).

Check sides at M: LM and MN. At Y: XY and YZ.

XYLM=84=2\dfrac{XY}{LM} = \dfrac{8}{4} = 2LMXY​=48​=2 and YZMN=126=2\dfrac{YZ}{MN} = \dfrac{12}{6} = 2MNYZ​=612​=2 ✓

Figure: In △PQR\triangle PQR△PQR, SSS is a point on QRQRQR such that PS⊥QRPS \perp QRPS⊥QR. Given ∠QPR=90°\angle QPR = 90°∠QPR=90°.

Prove △PQS∼△RPS\triangle PQS \sim \triangle RPS△PQS∼△RPS.

In △PQS and △RPS:
  ∠PSQ = ∠PSR = 90°   ... (PS ⊥ QR, given)
  ∠PQS = ∠RPS         ... (both = 90° − ∠QPS, since ∠QPR = 90°)
∴ △PQS ~ △RPS        ... (AA)

Note: ∠QPS+∠PQS=90°\angle QPS + \angle PQS = 90°∠QPS+∠PQS=90° (in △PQS\triangle PQS△PQS). Also ∠RPS+∠PRS=90°\angle RPS + \angle PRS = 90°∠RPS+∠PRS=90° (in △RPS\triangle RPS△RPS). But ∠PRS=∠PQS\angle PRS = \angle PQS∠PRS=∠PQS... wait, actually: ∠QPS+∠SPR=90°\angle QPS + \angle SPR = 90°∠QPS+∠SPR=90° (since ∠QPR=90°\angle QPR = 90°∠QPR=90°). So ∠PQS=90°−∠QPS\angle PQS = 90° - \angle QPS∠PQS=90°−∠QPS and ∠RPS=90°−∠QPS\angle RPS = 90° - \angle QPS∠RPS=90°−∠QPS. Equal! ✓

Figure: Two triangles with sides: △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC: 5, 7, 9. △PQR\triangle PQR△PQR: 10, 14, 18.

Are they similar? State the condition and scale factor.

Check ratios (sort both sets): 5/10? No — match large to large: 10/5=2, 14/7=2, 18/9=2. All equal.

SSS: △ABC∼△PQR\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR△ABC∼△PQR with k=2k = 2k=2.

Summary​

The complete picture
AASASSSS
Check2 angle pairs1 angle + 2 adjacent side ratios3 side ratios
Key wordCommon / corresponding / vertically oppositeIncluded angleAll three equal ratios
Exam frequency🔥 Most commonMediumLess common
Easiest mistakeWrong letter orderAngle not includedWrong side pairing
