4. The Three Conditions — AA, SAS, SSS

The central question of this chapter​

Given two triangles — how do you PROVE they are similar without measuring every angle and every side?

Mathematicians discovered that you don't need to check everything. Just three shortcuts work.

The three conditions

If any ONE of these is true, the triangles are similar:

  1. AA — Two pairs of angles are equal
  2. SAS — One pair of angles is equal AND the sides around that angle are proportional
  3. SSS — All three pairs of corresponding sides are proportional

Condition 1 — AA (Angle-Angle)​

The idea​

If two angles of one triangle equal two angles of another triangle — the triangles are similar.

Why only two angles? Because angles in a triangle always add up to 180°. So if two angles match, the third one is forced to match too (180° minus the other two).

∠A=∠P and ∠B=∠Q  ⟹  ∠C=∠R automatically\angle A = \angle P \text{ and } \angle B = \angle Q \implies \angle C = \angle R \text{ automatically}∠A=∠P and ∠B=∠Q⟹∠C=∠R automatically

Visualised​

When to use AA​

AA is the most commonly used condition in ICSE problems because:

  • You only need to spot 2 equal angles
  • Parallel lines automatically create equal corresponding angles (very common in figures)
  • A shared angle counts as a "free" equal angle

AA in practice​

  • Using Parallel Lines
  • Using a Shared Angle
  • Vertically Opposite Angles

Figure: In △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC, DE∥BCDE \parallel BCDE∥BC where DDD is on ABABAB and EEE is on ACACAC.

Why is △ADE∼△ABC\triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC△ADE∼△ABC?

  • ∠A=∠A\angle A = \angle A∠A=∠A — common angle (shared by both triangles)
  • ∠ADE=∠ABC\angle ADE = \angle ABC∠ADE=∠ABC — corresponding angles (since DE∥BCDE \parallel BCDE∥BC)

Two angles match → AA → △ADE∼△ABC\triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC△ADE∼△ABC

Figure: In △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC, ∠ADB=90°\angle ADB = 90°∠ADB=90° where DDD is the foot of altitude from AAA.

Why is △ABD∼△ACD\triangle ABD \sim \triangle ACD△ABD∼△ACD?

  • ∠ADB=∠ADC=90°\angle ADB = \angle ADC = 90°∠ADB=∠ADC=90°
  • ∠B=∠B\angle B = \angle B∠B=∠B in △ABD\triangle ABD△ABD, ∠A=∠A\angle A = \angle A∠A=∠A in △ACD\triangle ACD△ACD...

Actually: ∠ABD\angle ABD∠ABD in △ABD\triangle ABD△ABD and ∠DAC\angle DAC∠DAC in △ACD\triangle ACD△ACD — these are complementary pairs. A shared vertex creates common angles.

Figure: Diagonals of a quadrilateral intersect at OOO.

∠AOB=∠COD\angle AOB = \angle COD∠AOB=∠COD — vertically opposite angles (equal)

If also ∠OAB=∠OCD\angle OAB = \angle OCD∠OAB=∠OCD — alternate angles (if AB∥CDAB \parallel CDAB∥CD)

Then △AOB∼△COD\triangle AOB \sim \triangle COD△AOB∼△COD by AA.

Condition 2 — SAS (Side-Angle-Side)​

The idea​

One angle of the first triangle equals one angle of the second triangle, AND the two sides that form that angle are in the same ratio in both triangles.

∠A=∠PandABPQ=ACPR\angle A = \angle P \quad \text{and} \quad \dfrac{AB}{PQ} = \dfrac{AC}{PR}∠A=∠PandPQAB​=PRAC​

Then △ABC∼△PQR\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR△ABC∼△PQR.

The critical word: INCLUDED​

The equal angle must be the angle between (included by) the two proportional sides. An angle at one corner, with the two sides of that corner being the proportional ones.

SAS in practice​

  • Worked Example
  • ⚠️ The SAS Trap

In △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC and △PQR\triangle PQR△PQR:

  • ∠B=∠Q=60°\angle B = \angle Q = 60°∠B=∠Q=60°
  • AB=4AB = 4AB=4, PQ=6PQ = 6PQ=6
  • BC=6BC = 6BC=6, QR=9QR = 9QR=9

Check: PQAB=64=1.5\dfrac{PQ}{AB} = \dfrac{6}{4} = 1.5ABPQ​=46​=1.5 and QRBC=96=1.5\dfrac{QR}{BC} = \dfrac{9}{6} = 1.5BCQR​=69​=1.5 ✓

Equal ratio + included angle ∠B=∠Q\angle B = \angle Q∠B=∠Q

Conclusion: △ABC∼△PQR\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR△ABC∼△PQR by SAS with k=1.5k = 1.5k=1.5

What goes wrong: Students use SAS when the equal angle is NOT between the two proportional sides.

Example of wrong usage:

∠A=∠P\angle A = \angle P∠A=∠P (at vertex A and P), sides BC=5BC = 5BC=5 and QR=10QR = 10QR=10 (ratio 2), sides AB=3AB = 3AB=3 and PQ=6PQ = 6PQ=6 (ratio 2).

But wait — ∠A\angle A∠A is the angle at vertex AAA, which is between sides ABABAB and ACACAC. The side BCBCBC is NOT adjacent to ∠A\angle A∠A.

So BC/QRBC/QRBC/QR cannot be used for SAS with angle AAA.

Rule: Only use sides that meet at the equal angle.

Condition 3 — SSS (Side-Side-Side)​

The idea​

All three pairs of corresponding sides are in the same ratio.

ABPQ=BCQR=CARP=k\dfrac{AB}{PQ} = \dfrac{BC}{QR} = \dfrac{CA}{RP} = kPQAB​=QRBC​=RPCA​=k

If all three ratios are equal → the triangles are similar.

SSS in practice​

  • How to Check SSS
  • When SSS Fails

Step 1: List the sides of both triangles in order (smallest to largest or matched by position).

Step 2: Compute each ratio: neworiginal\dfrac{\text{new}}{\text{original}}originalnew​.

Step 3: If all three ratios are identical → SSS → Similar.

Example:

△ABC\triangle ABC△ABC: sides 5, 7, 9. △PQR\triangle PQR△PQR: sides 10, 14, 18.

10/5=210/5 = 210/5=2, 14/7=214/7 = 214/7=2, 18/9=218/9 = 218/9=2. All equal → SSS → △ABC∼△PQR\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR△ABC∼△PQR with k=2k = 2k=2.

Example:

△ABC\triangle ABC△ABC: sides 3, 4, 5. △PQR\triangle PQR△PQR: sides 6, 9, 10.

6/3=26/3 = 26/3=2, 9/4=2.259/4 = 2.259/4=2.25, 10/5=210/5 = 210/5=2.

The middle ratio is different. NOT similar.

Even one unequal ratio breaks similarity.

Comparing all three conditions​

ConditionWhat you checkMost common use
AA2 pairs of equal anglesParallel lines, shared angles, alternate angles
SAS1 equal angle + the 2 sides around it are proportionalWhen angles are given with some sides
SSSAll 3 side ratios equalWhen all sides are given, no angles
Which condition to use in an exam?
  • Parallel lines in the figure? → Almost always AA
  • One angle given, some sides given? → Try SAS
  • All sides given, no angles? → SSS
  • Two angles mentioned or provable? → AA (easiest)

Writing the proof — exam format​

ICSE expects you to state the condition clearly. Here is the exact format:

In △ABC and △PQR:
  ∠A = ∠P    ... (given / alternate angles / corresponding angles)
  ∠B = ∠Q    ... (give reason)
∴ △ABC ~ △PQR    ... (AA)

Always:

  1. Write the two triangles being compared
  2. State the equal angles / proportional sides
  3. Give a reason for each
  4. State the conclusion with the condition used

In △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC, DE∥BCDE \parallel BCDE∥BC (DDD on ABABAB, EEE on ACACAC). Write a similarity statement and state the condition.

In △ADE\triangle ADE△ADE and △ABC\triangle ABC△ABC:

  • ∠DAE=∠BAC\angle DAE = \angle BAC∠DAE=∠BAC (common angle at AAA)
  • ∠ADE=∠ABC\angle ADE = \angle ABC∠ADE=∠ABC (corresponding angles, DE∥BCDE \parallel BCDE∥BC)

∴△ADE∼△ABC\therefore \triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC∴△ADE∼△ABC (AA)

Note the order: AAA matches AAA, DDD matches BBB, EEE matches CCC.

△PQR\triangle PQR△PQR has PQ=6PQ = 6PQ=6, QR=8QR = 8QR=8, PR=10PR = 10PR=10. △XYZ\triangle XYZ△XYZ has XY=3XY = 3XY=3, YZ=4YZ = 4YZ=4, XZ=5XZ = 5XZ=5.

Are they similar? State the condition.

PQXY=63=2\dfrac{PQ}{XY} = \dfrac{6}{3} = 2XYPQ​=36​=2, QRYZ=84=2\quad \dfrac{QR}{YZ} = \dfrac{8}{4} = 2YZQR​=48​=2, PRXZ=105=2\quad \dfrac{PR}{XZ} = \dfrac{10}{5} = 2XZPR​=510​=2

All ratios equal 2. ∴△PQR∼△XYZ\therefore \triangle PQR \sim \triangle XYZ∴△PQR∼△XYZ (SSS) with k=2k = 2k=2.

Summary​

Remember
  • AA — 2 angles equal (third is automatic) — most common
  • SAS — 1 angle equal AND the sides AROUND that angle are proportional
  • SSS — all 3 side ratios equal
  • In ICSE: AA appears in 80% of questions — master it first
