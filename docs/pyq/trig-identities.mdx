# 📐 PYQ — Trigonometric Identities (2000–2025)

:::tip Before you start
**Two question types only:**
1. **Prove** — work ONE side only. Never cross the = sign.
2. **Evaluate** — spot complementary pairs first ($\cos 70° = \sin 20°$), then use standard values.
:::

## Year 2000

<PYQCard year="2000" marks="4" qnum="1">

Prove: $\sqrt{\dfrac{1-\cos A}{1+\cos A}} = \dfrac{\sin A}{1+\cos A}$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Square root fraction — rationalize by multiplying inside by the numerator.

**💡 TRICK:** When you see $\sqrt{\dfrac{a}{b}}$, multiply top and bottom inside by $a$ to make a perfect square in the numerator. Then the $\sqrt{}$ vanishes.

**Step 1 — Rationalize LHS:** Multiply inside $\sqrt{}$ by $\dfrac{1-\cos A}{1-\cos A}$:

$$\text{LHS} = \sqrt{\dfrac{(1-\cos A)^2}{(1+\cos A)(1-\cos A)}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{(1-\cos A)^2}{1-\cos^2 A}}$$

**Step 2 — Use identity 1:** $1-\cos^2 A = \sin^2 A$:

$$= \sqrt{\dfrac{(1-\cos A)^2}{\sin^2 A}} = \dfrac{1-\cos A}{\sin A}$$

**Step 3 — Show RHS equals same:** Multiply RHS top and bottom by $(1-\cos A)$:

$$\dfrac{\sin A}{1+\cos A} = \dfrac{\sin A(1-\cos A)}{1-\cos^2 A} = \dfrac{\sin A(1-\cos A)}{\sin^2 A} = \dfrac{1-\cos A}{\sin A} \checkmark$$

⚠️ **Common mistake:** Trying to simplify $\sqrt{\dfrac{1-\cos A}{1+\cos A}}$ directly. You can't — always rationalize first.

</details>

</PYQCard>

<PYQCard year="2000" marks="4" qnum="2">

Prove: $\dfrac{1}{1+\cos(90°-A)}+\dfrac{1}{1-\cos(90°-A)} = 2\operatorname{cosec}^2(90°-A)$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Complementary angle substitution.

**💡 TRICK:** Spot the $90°-A$ angles immediately. Replace them using $\cos(90°-A)=\sin A$ and $\operatorname{cosec}(90°-A)=\sec A$. After that it's just adding two fractions.

**Step 1 — Replace:** $\cos(90°-A)=\sin A$, $\operatorname{cosec}(90°-A)=\sec A$:

$$\text{LHS} = \dfrac{1}{1+\sin A}+\dfrac{1}{1-\sin A}$$

**Step 2 — Add fractions** (common denominator = $(1+\sin A)(1-\sin A) = 1-\sin^2 A$):

$$= \dfrac{(1-\sin A)+(1+\sin A)}{1-\sin^2 A} = \dfrac{2}{1-\sin^2 A}$$

**Step 3 — Use identity:** $1-\sin^2 A = \cos^2 A$:

$$= \dfrac{2}{\cos^2 A} = 2\sec^2 A = 2\operatorname{cosec}^2(90°-A) = \text{RHS} \checkmark$$

✅ **Exam tip:** Always do the $90°-A$ replacement as your very first step. Never leave it for later.

</details>

</PYQCard>

## Year 2001

<PYQCard year="2001" marks="4" qnum="1">

Prove: $\sqrt{\dfrac{1-\sin A}{1+\sin A}} = \dfrac{\cos A}{1+\sin A}$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Square root fraction — same method as 2000 Q1, but with $\sin A$ instead of $\cos A$.

**💡 TRICK:** Multiply inside $\sqrt{}$ by $\dfrac{1-\sin A}{1-\sin A}$ to create a perfect square. Then use $1-\sin^2 A = \cos^2 A$.

**Step 1:**

$$\text{LHS} = \sqrt{\dfrac{(1-\sin A)^2}{1-\sin^2 A}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{(1-\sin A)^2}{\cos^2 A}} = \dfrac{1-\sin A}{\cos A}$$

**Step 2 — Confirm RHS equals same:** $\dfrac{\cos A}{1+\sin A} \times \dfrac{1-\sin A}{1-\sin A} = \dfrac{\cos A(1-\sin A)}{\cos^2 A} = \dfrac{1-\sin A}{\cos A} \checkmark$

</details>

</PYQCard>

<PYQCard year="2001" marks="4" qnum="2">

If $2\sin A-1=0$, show that $\sin 3A = 3\sin A - 4\sin^3 A$.

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Find $A$ first, then verify both sides numerically.

**💡 TRICK:** This is NOT a proof — it's a verification. Find $A$ from the given equation, then check LHS = RHS by substituting.

**Step 1 — Find $A$:** $2\sin A=1 \Rightarrow \sin A=\dfrac{1}{2} \Rightarrow A=30°$

**Step 2 — Evaluate LHS:** $\sin 3A = \sin 90° = 1$

**Step 3 — Evaluate RHS:** 
$$3\sin A - 4\sin^3 A = 3\times\dfrac{1}{2} - 4\times\left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)^3 = \dfrac{3}{2} - 4\times\dfrac{1}{8} = \dfrac{3}{2} - \dfrac{1}{2} = 1 \checkmark$$

✅ **Exam tip:** Write "LHS = 1" and "RHS = 1" clearly. Then state "LHS = RHS, hence proved."

</details>

</PYQCard>

## Year 2002

<PYQCard year="2002" marks="4" qnum="1">

Prove: $\dfrac{1}{\sin A+\cos A}+\dfrac{1}{\sin A-\cos A} = \dfrac{2\sin A}{1-2\cos^2 A}$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Add two fractions whose denominators are conjugate pairs $(a+b)$ and $(a-b)$.

**💡 TRICK:** When you add $\dfrac{1}{a+b}+\dfrac{1}{a-b}$, the result is $\dfrac{2a}{a^2-b^2}$. Here $a=\sin A$, $b=\cos A$.

**Step 1 — Add fractions:**

$$\text{LHS} = \dfrac{(\sin A-\cos A)+(\sin A+\cos A)}{(\sin A+\cos A)(\sin A-\cos A)} = \dfrac{2\sin A}{\sin^2 A - \cos^2 A}$$

**Step 2 — Rewrite denominator:** $\sin^2 A - \cos^2 A = (1-\cos^2 A)-\cos^2 A = 1-2\cos^2 A$:

$$= \dfrac{2\sin A}{1-2\cos^2 A} = \text{RHS} \checkmark$$

✅ **Exam tip:** The denominator simplification in Step 2 is where most marks are. Show it clearly.

</details>

</PYQCard>

<PYQCard year="2002" marks="4" qnum="2">

Prove: $\dfrac{1}{1+\sin(90°-A)}+\dfrac{1}{1-\sin(90°-A)} = 2\sec^2(90°-A)$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Complementary angles — same structure as 2000 Q2.

**💡 TRICK:** Replace $\sin(90°-A)=\cos A$ and $\sec(90°-A)=\operatorname{cosec} A$ first.

**Step 1 — Replace:** LHS becomes $\dfrac{1}{1+\cos A}+\dfrac{1}{1-\cos A}$

**Step 2 — Add:** $= \dfrac{2}{1-\cos^2 A} = \dfrac{2}{\sin^2 A} = 2\operatorname{cosec}^2 A = 2\sec^2(90°-A) = \text{RHS} \checkmark$

</details>

</PYQCard>

## Year 2003

<PYQCard year="2003" marks="4" qnum="1">

Prove: $\dfrac{\cos A}{1-\tan A}+\dfrac{\sin A}{1-\cot A} = \sin A+\cos A$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Fractions with tan and cot — convert to sin/cos, then simplify.

**💡 TRICK:** Replace $\tan A = \dfrac{\sin A}{\cos A}$ and $\cot A = \dfrac{\cos A}{\sin A}$. The denominators will simplify beautifully.

**Step 1 — Replace tan and cot:**

$$= \dfrac{\cos A}{1-\frac{\sin A}{\cos A}}+\dfrac{\sin A}{1-\frac{\cos A}{\sin A}} = \dfrac{\cos A}{\frac{\cos A - \sin A}{\cos A}}+\dfrac{\sin A}{\frac{\sin A-\cos A}{\sin A}}$$

**Step 2 — Simplify each fraction:**

$$= \dfrac{\cos^2 A}{\cos A-\sin A}+\dfrac{\sin^2 A}{\sin A-\cos A} = \dfrac{\cos^2 A}{\cos A-\sin A}-\dfrac{\sin^2 A}{\cos A-\sin A}$$

**Step 3 — Combine** (same denominator now):

$$= \dfrac{\cos^2 A - \sin^2 A}{\cos A-\sin A} = \dfrac{(\cos A+\sin A)(\cos A-\sin A)}{\cos A-\sin A} = \cos A+\sin A = \text{RHS} \checkmark$$

⚠️ **Common mistake:** In Step 2, $\sin A-\cos A = -(\cos A-\sin A)$ — don't miss that sign change.

✅ **Exam tip:** The factor $(cosA+sinA)(cosA-sinA)$ cancelling the denominator is the key step. Show it.

</details>

</PYQCard>

## Year 2004

<PYQCard year="2004" marks="4" qnum="1">

Prove: $\dfrac{\cot A+\tan B}{\cot B+\tan A} = \cot A\tan B$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Mixed cot/tan fraction — convert all to sin/cos.

**💡 TRICK:** Replace everything: $\cot A=\dfrac{\cos A}{\sin A}$, $\tan B=\dfrac{\sin B}{\cos B}$, etc. After adding the fractions in numerator and denominator, a big expression will **cancel** — that's the magic.

**Step 1 — Replace and add numerator:**

$$\cot A + \tan B = \dfrac{\cos A}{\sin A}+\dfrac{\sin B}{\cos B} = \dfrac{\cos A\cos B+\sin A\sin B}{\sin A\cos B}$$

**Step 2 — Replace and add denominator:**

$$\cot B + \tan A = \dfrac{\cos B}{\sin B}+\dfrac{\sin A}{\cos A} = \dfrac{\cos A\cos B+\sin A\sin B}{\sin B\cos A}$$

**Step 3 — Divide** (the numerator of the fraction, $\cos A\cos B+\sin A\sin B$, **cancels**):

$$\text{LHS} = \dfrac{\cos A\cos B+\sin A\sin B}{\sin A\cos B} \div \dfrac{\cos A\cos B+\sin A\sin B}{\sin B\cos A} = \dfrac{\sin B\cos A}{\sin A\cos B} = \dfrac{\cos A}{\sin A}\times\dfrac{\sin B}{\cos B} = \cot A\tan B = \text{RHS} \checkmark$$

✅ **Exam tip:** The cancellation in Step 3 is the key. Once you see the same expression on top and bottom, write "cancels" explicitly.

</details>

</PYQCard>

<PYQCard year="2004" marks="3" qnum="2">

Evaluate: $14\sin 30°+6\cos 60°-5\tan 45°$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Standard angle evaluation — write values first, substitute, calculate.

**Step 1 — Write standard values:**

$$\sin 30°=\dfrac{1}{2}, \quad \cos 60°=\dfrac{1}{2}, \quad \tan 45°=1$$

**Step 2 — Substitute:**

$$= 14\times\dfrac{1}{2}+6\times\dfrac{1}{2}-5\times 1 = 7+3-5 = \boxed{5}$$

✅ **Exam tip:** Always write the standard value separately before substituting. Examiners award 1 mark just for the correct values.

</details>

</PYQCard>

## Year 2005

<PYQCard year="2005" marks="4" qnum="1">

Prove: $(1-\tan A)^2+(1+\tan A)^2 = 2\sec^2 A$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Algebraic expansion — use $(a-b)^2+(a+b)^2 = 2a^2+2b^2$.

**💡 TRICK:** You do NOT need to expand fully. Recall: $(a-b)^2+(a+b)^2 = 2(a^2+b^2)$. Here $a=1$, $b=\tan A$.

**Step 1 — Apply the shortcut:**

$$(1-\tan A)^2+(1+\tan A)^2 = 2(1^2+\tan^2 A) = 2(1+\tan^2 A)$$

**Step 2 — Use identity 2:** $1+\tan^2 A = \sec^2 A$:

$$= 2\sec^2 A = \text{RHS} \checkmark$$

⚠️ **Common mistake:** Expanding fully and spending 3 minutes. Use the shortcut.

</details>

</PYQCard>

## Year 2006

<PYQCard year="2006" marks="4" qnum="1">

Prove: $(1+\tan^2 A)+\left(1+\dfrac{1}{\tan^2 A}\right) = \dfrac{1}{\sin^2 A-\sin^4 A}$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Convert using identities, then simplify RHS.

**💡 TRICK:** $1+\tan^2 A = \sec^2 A$ and $1+\cot^2 A = \operatorname{cosec}^2 A$ (note: $\dfrac{1}{\tan^2 A} = \cot^2 A$). Then add sec² + cosec².

**Step 1 — Simplify LHS:**

$$\text{LHS} = \sec^2 A + (1+\cot^2 A) = \sec^2 A + \operatorname{cosec}^2 A = \dfrac{1}{\cos^2 A}+\dfrac{1}{\sin^2 A}$$

**Step 2 — Add fractions:**

$$= \dfrac{\sin^2 A+\cos^2 A}{\sin^2 A\cos^2 A} = \dfrac{1}{\sin^2 A\cos^2 A}$$

**Step 3 — Simplify RHS:**

$$\dfrac{1}{\sin^2 A-\sin^4 A} = \dfrac{1}{\sin^2 A(1-\sin^2 A)} = \dfrac{1}{\sin^2 A\cdot\cos^2 A} = \text{LHS} \checkmark$$

✅ **Exam tip:** Work both sides toward each other — LHS from left, RHS from right, meet in the middle.

</details>

</PYQCard>

## Year 2007

<PYQCard year="2007" marks="4" qnum="1">

Prove: $\dfrac{1}{\sec A+\tan A} = \sec A-\tan A$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Rationalize using conjugate — the identity $\sec^2 A-\tan^2 A=1$ does the work.

**💡 TRICK:** Whenever you see $\dfrac{1}{\sec A+\tan A}$, multiply top and bottom by $(\sec A-\tan A)$. The denominator becomes $\sec^2 A-\tan^2 A=1$ and vanishes.

**Step 1 — Multiply by conjugate:**

$$\text{LHS} = \dfrac{1}{\sec A+\tan A}\times\dfrac{\sec A-\tan A}{\sec A-\tan A} = \dfrac{\sec A-\tan A}{\sec^2 A-\tan^2 A}$$

**Step 2 — Use identity 2:** $\sec^2 A-\tan^2 A=1$:

$$= \dfrac{\sec A-\tan A}{1} = \sec A-\tan A = \text{RHS} \checkmark$$

✅ **Exam tip:** This exact question (or its mirror $\dfrac{1}{\sec A-\tan A}=\sec A+\tan A$) appears very frequently. Memorise the conjugate trick.

</details>

</PYQCard>

<PYQCard year="2007" marks="3" qnum="2">

Evaluate: $\dfrac{\cos 70°}{\sin 20°}+\dfrac{\cos 59°}{\sin 31°}-8\sin^2 30°$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Complementary angle evaluation — each fraction collapses to 1.

**💡 TRICK:** For every fraction, check if numerator angle + denominator angle = 90°. If yes, the fraction = 1.

**Step 1 — Check pairs:**
- $70°+20°=90°$ so $\cos 70°=\sin 20°$ → first fraction $=\dfrac{\sin 20°}{\sin 20°}=1$
- $59°+31°=90°$ so $\cos 59°=\sin 31°$ → second fraction $=1$

**Step 2 — Standard value:** $\sin 30°=\dfrac{1}{2}$, so $8\sin^2 30°=8\times\dfrac{1}{4}=2$

**Step 3:** $= 1+1-2 = \boxed{0}$

</details>

</PYQCard>

## Year 2008

<PYQCard year="2008" marks="4" qnum="1">

Prove: $\dfrac{\sin A\tan A}{1-\cos A} = 1+\sec A$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Simplify fraction — use $\sin^2 A = 1-\cos^2 A = (1-\cos A)(1+\cos A)$ to factor.

**💡 TRICK:** Replace $\tan A = \dfrac{\sin A}{\cos A}$. You get $\dfrac{\sin^2 A}{\cos A(1-\cos A)}$. Now replace $\sin^2 A$ with $(1-\cos A)(1+\cos A)$ and the $(1-\cos A)$ cancels.

**Step 1 — Replace $\tan A$:**

$$\text{LHS} = \dfrac{\sin A \cdot \frac{\sin A}{\cos A}}{1-\cos A} = \dfrac{\sin^2 A}{\cos A(1-\cos A)}$$

**Step 2 — Replace $\sin^2 A$:**

$$= \dfrac{(1-\cos A)(1+\cos A)}{\cos A(1-\cos A)}$$

**Step 3 — Cancel $(1-\cos A)$:**

$$= \dfrac{1+\cos A}{\cos A} = \dfrac{1}{\cos A}+1 = \sec A+1 = \text{RHS} \checkmark$$

✅ **Exam tip:** The trick of replacing $\sin^2 A = (1-\cos A)(1+\cos A)$ to cancel a factor appears in many questions. Recognise it instantly.

</details>

</PYQCard>

<PYQCard year="2010" marks="3" qnum="1">

Evaluate: $\sin 35°\cos 55°+\cos 35°\sin 55°$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Complementary angles — but also recognise the compound angle formula.

**💡 TRICK:** $35°+55°=90°$, so $\cos 55°=\sin 35°$ and $\sin 55°=\cos 35°$.

$$= \sin 35°\times\sin 35°+\cos 35°\times\cos 35° = \sin^2 35°+\cos^2 35° = \boxed{1}$$

**Faster trick:** This is $\sin(35°+55°)=\sin 90°=1$ by the compound angle formula.

</details>

</PYQCard>

Evaluate: $\operatorname{cosec}^2 10°-\tan^2 80°$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Complementary angle + identity.

**💡 TRICK:** $10°+80°=90°$, so $\tan 80°=\cot 10°$. Then use identity 3.

$$= \operatorname{cosec}^2 10°-\cot^2 10° = \boxed{1}$$

(Because $\operatorname{cosec}^2 A-\cot^2 A=1$ for any angle $A$.)

</details>

</PYQCard>

<PYQCard year="2011" marks="4" qnum="2">

Prove: $(\operatorname{cosec}A-\sin A)(\sec A-\cos A)\sec^2 A = \tan A$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Product of three brackets — simplify each bracket first.

**💡 TRICK:** Each bracket simplifies to a simple fraction when you replace cosec and sec with 1/sin and 1/cos.

**Step 1 — Simplify each bracket:**

$$\operatorname{cosec}A-\sin A = \dfrac{1}{\sin A}-\sin A = \dfrac{1-\sin^2 A}{\sin A} = \dfrac{\cos^2 A}{\sin A}$$

$$\sec A-\cos A = \dfrac{1}{\cos A}-\cos A = \dfrac{1-\cos^2 A}{\cos A} = \dfrac{\sin^2 A}{\cos A}$$

$$\sec^2 A = \dfrac{1}{\cos^2 A}$$

**Step 2 — Multiply all three:**

$$= \dfrac{\cos^2 A}{\sin A}\times\dfrac{\sin^2 A}{\cos A}\times\dfrac{1}{\cos^2 A} = \dfrac{\cos^2 A\cdot\sin^2 A}{\sin A\cdot\cos A\cdot\cos^2 A} = \dfrac{\sin A}{\cos A} = \tan A = \text{RHS} \checkmark$$

⚠️ **Common mistake:** Multiplying all three brackets out without simplifying first. Always simplify each bracket individually first.

</details>

</PYQCard>

## Year 2012

<PYQCard year="2012" marks="4" qnum="1">

Prove: $\dfrac{1+\cos A}{1-\cos A} = \dfrac{\tan^2 A}{(\sec A-1)^2}$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Work from RHS — it simplifies to LHS more cleanly.

**💡 TRICK:** $\tan^2 A = \sec^2 A-1 = (\sec A+1)(\sec A-1)$. This factors beautifully to cancel the $(\sec A-1)$ in the denominator.

**Step 1 — Factor numerator of RHS:**

$$\text{RHS} = \dfrac{\tan^2 A}{(\sec A-1)^2} = \dfrac{\sec^2 A-1}{(\sec A-1)^2} = \dfrac{(\sec A+1)(\sec A-1)}{(\sec A-1)^2} = \dfrac{\sec A+1}{\sec A-1}$$

**Step 2 — Replace $\sec A = \dfrac{1}{\cos A}$:**

$$= \dfrac{\frac{1}{\cos A}+1}{\frac{1}{\cos A}-1} = \dfrac{\frac{1+\cos A}{\cos A}}{\frac{1-\cos A}{\cos A}} = \dfrac{1+\cos A}{1-\cos A} = \text{LHS} \checkmark$$

✅ **Exam tip:** When LHS looks simpler, sometimes it's easier to work from RHS. This is allowed.

</details>

</PYQCard>

## Year 2014

<PYQCard year="2014" marks="3" qnum="1">

Evaluate: $\sin^2 34°+\sin^2 56°+2\tan 18°\tan 72°-\cot^2 30°$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Evaluate using complementary angles and standard values.

**💡 TRICK:** Handle each part separately. Identify the complementary pair, the product pair, and the standard value.

**Part 1:** $56°=90°-34°$, so $\sin 56°=\cos 34°$:
$$\sin^2 34°+\sin^2 56° = \sin^2 34°+\cos^2 34° = 1$$

**Part 2:** $72°=90°-18°$, so $\tan 72°=\cot 18°$:
$$2\tan 18°\times\tan 72° = 2\tan 18°\times\cot 18° = 2\times 1 = 2$$

**Part 3:** $\cot 30°=\sqrt{3}$, so $\cot^2 30°=3$

**Total:** $= 1+2-3 = \boxed{0}$

✅ **Exam tip:** Lay out all three parts separately. Showing your reasoning for each part earns step marks.

</details>

</PYQCard>

<PYQCard year="2014" marks="4" qnum="2">

Prove: $(\sin\theta+\cos\theta)(\tan\theta+\cot\theta) = \sec\theta+\operatorname{cosec}\theta$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Product — simplify the second bracket first.

**💡 TRICK:** $\tan\theta+\cot\theta = \dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}+\dfrac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta} = \dfrac{\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta}{\sin\theta\cos\theta} = \dfrac{1}{\sin\theta\cos\theta}$.

Once you see this, the rest is straightforward.

**Step 1 — Simplify second bracket:**

$$\tan\theta+\cot\theta = \dfrac{1}{\sin\theta\cos\theta}$$

**Step 2 — Multiply:**

$$\text{LHS} = (\sin\theta+\cos\theta)\times\dfrac{1}{\sin\theta\cos\theta} = \dfrac{\sin\theta+\cos\theta}{\sin\theta\cos\theta}$$

**Step 3 — Split the fraction:**

$$= \dfrac{\sin\theta}{\sin\theta\cos\theta}+\dfrac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta\cos\theta} = \dfrac{1}{\cos\theta}+\dfrac{1}{\sin\theta} = \sec\theta+\operatorname{cosec}\theta = \text{RHS} \checkmark$$

✅ **Exam tip:** Memorise that $\tan A+\cot A = \dfrac{1}{\sin A\cos A}$. It appears very often.

</details>

</PYQCard>

Evaluate: $\operatorname{csc}^2 57°-\tan^2 33°+\cos 44°\cdot\operatorname{csc}46°-\sqrt{2}\cos 45°-\tan^2 60°$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Multi-part evaluation — handle each term separately.

**Term 1:** $57°+33°=90°$, so $\csc 57°=\sec 33°$ and $\tan 33°=\cot 57°$:
$$\operatorname{csc}^2 57°-\tan^2 33° = \operatorname{csc}^2 57°-\cot^2 57° = 1$$

**Term 2:** $44°+46°=90°$, so $\sin 46°=\cos 44°$:
$$\cos 44°\cdot\operatorname{csc}46° = \dfrac{\cos 44°}{\sin 46°} = \dfrac{\cos 44°}{\cos 44°} = 1$$

**Term 3:** $\sqrt{2}\cos 45°=\sqrt{2}\times\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}=1$

**Term 4:** $\tan^2 60°=(\sqrt{3})^2=3$

**Total:** $= 1+1-1-3 = \boxed{-2}$

</details>

Prove: $\dfrac{\cos A}{1+\sin A}+\tan A = \sec A$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Combine fractions — find common denominator.

**💡 TRICK:** Replace $\tan A=\dfrac{\sin A}{\cos A}$. Common denominator is $\cos A(1+\sin A)$.

**Step 1 — Replace and find common denominator:**

$$\text{LHS} = \dfrac{\cos A}{1+\sin A}+\dfrac{\sin A}{\cos A} = \dfrac{\cos^2 A+\sin A(1+\sin A)}{\cos A(1+\sin A)}$$

**Step 2 — Expand numerator:**

$$= \dfrac{\cos^2 A+\sin A+\sin^2 A}{\cos A(1+\sin A)} = \dfrac{1+\sin A}{\cos A(1+\sin A)}$$

**Step 3 — Cancel $(1+\sin A)$:**

$$= \dfrac{1}{\cos A} = \sec A = \text{RHS} \checkmark$$

</details>

Prove: $\dfrac{\sin\theta-2\sin^3\theta}{2\cos^3\theta-\cos\theta} = \tan\theta$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Factorise numerator and denominator, then cancel.

**💡 TRICK:** Factor out $\sin\theta$ from numerator and $\cos\theta$ from denominator. You'll get $(1-2\sin^2\theta)$ in both — which cancels.

**Step 1 — Factorise:**

$$\text{LHS} = \dfrac{\sin\theta(1-2\sin^2\theta)}{\cos\theta(2\cos^2\theta-1)}$$

**Step 2 — Show denominators are equal:** Replace $\cos^2\theta=1-\sin^2\theta$:

$$2\cos^2\theta-1 = 2(1-\sin^2\theta)-1 = 1-2\sin^2\theta$$

**Step 3 — Cancel $(1-2\sin^2\theta)$:**

$$= \dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta} = \tan\theta = \text{RHS} \checkmark$$

✅ **Exam tip:** Step 2 (showing the two expressions are equal) is the key. Write it explicitly — that's where marks are awarded.

</details>

</PYQCard>

<PYQCard year="2018" marks="4" qnum="1">

Prove: $\sqrt{\sec^2\theta+\operatorname{cosec}^2\theta} = \tan\theta+\cot\theta$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Square root — square both sides to compare (valid since both sides are positive for $0°<\theta<90°$).

**💡 TRICK:** $\tan\theta+\cot\theta = \dfrac{1}{\sin\theta\cos\theta}$ (we saw this in 2014). Squaring gives $\dfrac{1}{\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta}$. Show LHS² equals the same.

**Step 1 — Square RHS:**

$$\text{RHS}^2 = (\tan\theta+\cot\theta)^2 = \left(\dfrac{1}{\sin\theta\cos\theta}\right)^2 = \dfrac{1}{\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta}$$

**Step 2 — Square LHS:**

$$\text{LHS}^2 = \sec^2\theta+\operatorname{cosec}^2\theta = \dfrac{1}{\cos^2\theta}+\dfrac{1}{\sin^2\theta} = \dfrac{\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta}{\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta} = \dfrac{1}{\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta}$$

**Step 3:** $\text{LHS}^2=\text{RHS}^2$ and both sides positive, so $\text{LHS}=\text{RHS}$ $\checkmark$

</details>

</PYQCard>

<PYQCard year="2018" marks="4" qnum="2">

Prove: $(1+\cot\theta-\operatorname{cosec}\theta)(1+\tan\theta+\sec\theta) = 2$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Product of two brackets — convert each to sin/cos with common denominator.

**💡 TRICK:** Write each bracket as a single fraction over $\sin\theta$ and $\cos\theta$ respectively. Then multiply. The numerators form a difference of squares pattern.

**Step 1 — Write as single fractions:**

$$1+\cot\theta-\operatorname{cosec}\theta = 1+\dfrac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}-\dfrac{1}{\sin\theta} = \dfrac{\sin\theta+\cos\theta-1}{\sin\theta}$$

$$1+\tan\theta+\sec\theta = 1+\dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}+\dfrac{1}{\cos\theta} = \dfrac{\cos\theta+\sin\theta+1}{\cos\theta}$$

**Step 2 — Multiply:**

$$\text{LHS} = \dfrac{(\sin\theta+\cos\theta-1)(\sin\theta+\cos\theta+1)}{\sin\theta\cos\theta}$$

**Step 3 — Use $(a-b)(a+b)=a^2-b^2$** where $a=\sin\theta+\cos\theta$, $b=1$:

$$= \dfrac{(\sin\theta+\cos\theta)^2-1}{\sin\theta\cos\theta} = \dfrac{\sin^2\theta+2\sin\theta\cos\theta+\cos^2\theta-1}{\sin\theta\cos\theta} = \dfrac{1+2\sin\theta\cos\theta-1}{\sin\theta\cos\theta} = \dfrac{2\sin\theta\cos\theta}{\sin\theta\cos\theta} = 2 \checkmark$$

✅ **Exam tip:** Step 3 uses $(\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta)=1$ invisibly. Write "using identity 1" next to it.

</details>

</PYQCard>

<PYQCard year="2019" marks="4" qnum="1">

Prove: $(\operatorname{cosec}\theta-\sin\theta)(\sec\theta-\cos\theta)(\tan\theta+\cot\theta) = 1$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Product of three brackets — simplify each bracket first (same method as 2011 Q2).

**Step 1 — Simplify each bracket:**

$$\operatorname{cosec}\theta-\sin\theta = \dfrac{1-\sin^2\theta}{\sin\theta} = \dfrac{\cos^2\theta}{\sin\theta}$$

$$\sec\theta-\cos\theta = \dfrac{1-\cos^2\theta}{\cos\theta} = \dfrac{\sin^2\theta}{\cos\theta}$$

$$\tan\theta+\cot\theta = \dfrac{1}{\sin\theta\cos\theta}$$

**Step 2 — Multiply all three:**

$$= \dfrac{\cos^2\theta}{\sin\theta}\times\dfrac{\sin^2\theta}{\cos\theta}\times\dfrac{1}{\sin\theta\cos\theta} = \dfrac{\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta}{\sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta} = 1 = \text{RHS} \checkmark$$

✅ **Exam tip:** This 3-bracket type follows the same pattern every time. Simplify each bracket to $\dfrac{\text{trig}^2}{\text{trig}}$ form, then everything cancels.

</details>

</PYQCard>

<PYQCard year="2020" marks="4" qnum="1">

Prove: $\left(\dfrac{1-\tan\theta}{1-\cot\theta}\right)^2 = \tan^2\theta$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Algebraic manipulation — convert $\cot\theta$ in terms of $\tan\theta$.

**💡 TRICK:** $1-\cot\theta = 1-\dfrac{1}{\tan\theta} = \dfrac{\tan\theta-1}{\tan\theta}$.

This means $1-\cot\theta = -\dfrac{(1-\tan\theta)}{\tan\theta}$. The $(1-\tan\theta)$ cancels with the numerator.

**Step 1 — Replace $\cot\theta$:**

$$1-\cot\theta = 1-\dfrac{1}{\tan\theta} = \dfrac{\tan\theta-1}{\tan\theta} = -\dfrac{(1-\tan\theta)}{\tan\theta}$$

**Step 2 — Substitute:**

$$\text{LHS} = \left(\dfrac{1-\tan\theta}{-\frac{1-\tan\theta}{\tan\theta}}\right)^2 = \left(\dfrac{(1-\tan\theta)\times\tan\theta}{-(1-\tan\theta)}\right)^2 = (-\tan\theta)^2 = \tan^2\theta = \text{RHS} \checkmark$$

</details>

</PYQCard>

Prove: $(\sin^2\theta-1)(\tan^2\theta+1)+1 = 0$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Direct identity substitution — the fastest prove question in the paper.

**💡 TRICK:** $\sin^2\theta-1 = -\cos^2\theta$ (from identity 1) and $\tan^2\theta+1 = \sec^2\theta$ (identity 2). Substitute both immediately.

**Step 1:** $(\sin^2\theta-1)(\tan^2\theta+1)+1 = (-\cos^2\theta)(\sec^2\theta)+1$

**Step 2:** $= -\cos^2\theta\times\dfrac{1}{\cos^2\theta}+1 = -1+1 = 0 = \text{RHS} \checkmark$

✅ **Exam tip:** This should take 30 seconds. Spot $\sin^2\theta-1$ and immediately write $-\cos^2\theta$. Done.

</details>

<PYQCard year="2024" marks="4" qnum="1">

Prove: $\dfrac{\sin^3\theta+\cos^3\theta}{\sin\theta+\cos\theta}+\sin\theta\cos\theta = 1$

<details>
<summary>Show Solution</summary>

**🔍 TYPE:** Cubic — use the factorisation $a^3+b^3=(a+b)(a^2-ab+b^2)$.

**💡 TRICK:** Once you apply the $a^3+b^3$ formula, $(\sin\theta+\cos\theta)$ cancels. What remains is $\sin^2\theta-\sin\theta\cos\theta+\cos^2\theta = 1-\sin\theta\cos\theta$. Adding $\sin\theta\cos\theta$ gives 1.

**Step 1 — Factorise numerator** using $a^3+b^3=(a+b)(a^2-ab+b^2)$:

$$\sin^3\theta+\cos^3\theta = (\sin\theta+\cos\theta)(\sin^2\theta-\sin\theta\cos\theta+\cos^2\theta)$$

**Step 2 — Cancel $(\sin\theta+\cos\theta)$:**

$$\text{LHS} = (\sin^2\theta-\sin\theta\cos\theta+\cos^2\theta)+\sin\theta\cos\theta$$

**Step 3 — Use identity 1** ($\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta=1$):

$$= 1-\sin\theta\cos\theta+\sin\theta\cos\theta = 1 = \text{RHS} \checkmark$$

✅ **Exam tip:** Write the $a^3+b^3$ formula explicitly before applying it. That's 1 mark for method even if you make a small error later.

</details>

</PYQCard>
