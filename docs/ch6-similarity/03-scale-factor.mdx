# 3. Scale Factor — The Zoom Number

## The one idea that drives everything

When two triangles are similar, there is one number — the **scale factor k** — that connects them completely.

> **Scale factor k = (any side of the new triangle) ÷ (corresponding side of the original triangle)**

It doesn't matter which pair of sides you use — you'll always get the same answer k.

## Three types of scale factor

<Tabs>
  <TabItem value="enlarge" label="k > 1 (Enlargement)">

When $k > 1$, the new triangle is **bigger** than the original.

**Example:** $\triangle ABC$ has sides 3, 4, 5. $k = 3$.

New triangle sides: $3 \times 3 = 9$,  $3 \times 4 = 12$,  $3 \times 5 = 15$.

$$\dfrac{9}{3} = \dfrac{12}{4} = \dfrac{15}{5} = 3 = k \checkmark$$

**Real life:** Zooming in on a map. Making a model bigger than the original design.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="reduce" label="k < 1 (Reduction)">

When $k < 1$, the new triangle is **smaller** than the original.

**Example:** $\triangle ABC$ has sides 10, 8, 6. $k = 0.5$.

New triangle sides: $0.5 \times 10 = 5$,  $0.5 \times 8 = 4$,  $0.5 \times 6 = 3$.

$$\dfrac{5}{10} = \dfrac{4}{8} = \dfrac{3}{6} = 0.5 = k \checkmark$$

**Real life:** A map where 1 cm = 2 km. Making a model of a building (much smaller than reality).

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="same" label="k = 1 (Congruence)">

When $k = 1$, the new shape is **identical** to the original — they are congruent.

**Example:** $\triangle ABC$ has sides 5, 7, 9. $k = 1$.

New triangle sides: $1 \times 5 = 5$,  $1 \times 7 = 7$,  $1 \times 9 = 9$.

Same triangle. Congruent.

**This proves:** Every congruent figure is similar with $k = 1$.

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## How to find k (the most important skill)

:::note Rule
To find the scale factor, divide **any** corresponding side of the new shape by the original shape.

$$k = \dfrac{\text{new side}}{\text{original side}}$$

**Important:** Always divide new ÷ original, not original ÷ new.
:::

**But wait** — how do I know which sides "correspond"?

The corresponding sides are the ones **opposite equal angles**. In $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR$:
- Side $BC$ (opposite $\angle A$) corresponds to side $QR$ (opposite $\angle P$, since $\angle A = \angle P$)
- Side $CA$ (opposite $\angle B$) corresponds to side $RP$ (opposite $\angle Q$)
- Side $AB$ (opposite $\angle C$) corresponds to side $PQ$ (opposite $\angle R$)

---

## Finding unknown sides using k

This is the main calculation skill for ICSE similarity questions.

**Method:**
1. Identify that two triangles are similar and write the correspondence
2. Find k using a pair of sides you know completely
3. Use k to find the unknown side

<Tabs>
  <TabItem value="example1" label="Example 1 — Basic">

$\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR$. $AB = 4$, $PQ = 10$, $BC = 6$. Find $QR$.

**Step 1 — Find k:**
$$k = \dfrac{PQ}{AB} = \dfrac{10}{4} = 2.5$$

**Step 2 — Find QR:**
$$\dfrac{QR}{BC} = k \Rightarrow QR = k \times BC = 2.5 \times 6 = 15$$

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="example2" label="Example 2 — Find Original">

$\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR$ with $k = 3$. $QR = 18$. Find $BC$.

Here we know the **new** side and need the **original**.

Since $k = \dfrac{QR}{BC}$:
$$BC = \dfrac{QR}{k} = \dfrac{18}{3} = 6$$

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="example3" label="Example 3 — Mixed">

In $\triangle DEF \sim \triangle XYZ$: $DE = 6$, $EF = 8$, $XY = 9$. Find $YZ$ and the scale factor.

**Step 1 — Find k:**
$$k = \dfrac{XY}{DE} = \dfrac{9}{6} = 1.5$$

**Step 2 — Find YZ** (corresponds to EF):
$$YZ = k \times EF = 1.5 \times 8 = 12$$

**Check:** $\dfrac{YZ}{EF} = \dfrac{12}{8} = 1.5 = k$ ✓

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## The proportionality equation

When triangles are similar, the corresponding sides form a **proportion**. You can write it as:

$$\dfrac{AB}{PQ} = \dfrac{BC}{QR} = \dfrac{CA}{RP}$$

This is one equation in disguise — all three fractions equal k.

**Cross-multiplying** gives you ways to find unknowns:

$$AB \times QR = PQ \times BC \quad \text{(from the first two fractions)}$$

:::warning Do not mix up the order
Always write: $\dfrac{\text{side from } \triangle 1}{\text{side from } \triangle 2} = \dfrac{\text{side from } \triangle 1}{\text{side from } \triangle 2}$

Never mix: $\dfrac{AB}{QR}$ — this puts sides from both triangles in the same fraction. That's wrong.
:::

---

## What scale factor does NOT tell you

The scale factor $k$ tells you about **lengths**. But:
- **Angles** are not multiplied by k — they stay the same
- **Area** is not multiplied by k — it's multiplied by k² (we'll cover this later)
- **Perimeter** IS multiplied by k — since all sides scale by k

---

## Summary

:::tip Key formulas
$$k = \dfrac{\text{new side}}{\text{original side}} \quad \text{(same for all corresponding pairs)}$$

| Feature | Scales by |
|---------|----------|
| Each side | k |
| Perimeter | k |
| Area | k² (later chapter) |
| Each angle | 1 (unchanged) |
:::
