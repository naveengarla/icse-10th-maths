# 6. Basic Proportionality Theorem (BPT)

## One theorem, two names

This theorem is known by two names:
- **Basic Proportionality Theorem (BPT)** — the official ICSE name
- **Thales' Theorem** — named after the ancient Greek mathematician

Both refer to exactly the same result.

---

## The theorem — what it says

:::note Statement of BPT
If a line is drawn **parallel to one side** of a triangle, it divides the **other two sides in the same ratio**.

In $\triangle ABC$, if $DE \parallel BC$ (where $D$ is on $AB$ and $E$ is on $AC$), then:

$$\dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}$$
:::

---

## Why does this work? The intuition

Imagine stretching a rubber band from vertex $A$ down to $BC$. Any horizontal slice through the band cuts it in the same proportion — because you're scaling everything equally from point $A$.

More formally: $DE \parallel BC$ means $\triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC$ (by AA). The similarity gives proportional sides, which gives the ratio result.

---

## The two forms — don't confuse them

<BPTWhy />

**Form 1 (the BPT ratio):**
$$\dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}$$
*Ratio of the two parts of each side*

**Form 2 (from similarity):**
$$\dfrac{AD}{AB} = \dfrac{AE}{AC} = \dfrac{DE}{BC}$$
*Ratio of part to the whole side*

Both are useful. Form 2 lets you find $DE$.

:::warning The most common error
Students mix up Form 1 and Form 2.

$\dfrac{AD}{DB}$ is NOT the same as $\dfrac{AD}{AB}$.

If $AD = 3$ and $DB = 5$:
- $\dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{3}{5}$ (Form 1)
- $\dfrac{AD}{AB} = \dfrac{3}{8}$ (Form 2 — using the whole side $AB = 3+5 = 8$)
:::

---

## Converse of BPT

The converse is also true and equally important:

:::note Converse of BPT
If a line divides two sides of a triangle in the same ratio, then that line is parallel to the third side.

If $\dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}$, then $DE \parallel BC$.
:::

Use the converse when you need to **prove a line is parallel**.

---

## Worked examples

<Tabs>
  <TabItem value="ex1" label="Find a length">

**Q:** In $\triangle ABC$, $DE \parallel BC$. $AD = 4$, $DB = 6$, $AE = 5$. Find $EC$.

**Solution:**

By BPT: $\dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}$

$$\dfrac{4}{6} = \dfrac{5}{EC}$$

$$EC = \dfrac{5 \times 6}{4} = \dfrac{30}{4} = 7.5$$

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="ex2" label="Find DE">

**Q:** In $\triangle ABC$, $DE \parallel BC$. $AD = 3$, $AB = 9$, $BC = 12$. Find $DE$.

**Solution:**

Using Form 2 (part to whole): $\dfrac{AD}{AB} = \dfrac{DE}{BC}$

$$\dfrac{3}{9} = \dfrac{DE}{12}$$

$$DE = \dfrac{3 \times 12}{9} = 4$$

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="ex3" label="Use the Converse">

**Q:** In $\triangle PQR$, $D$ is on $PQ$ and $E$ is on $PR$ with $PD = 4$, $DQ = 8$, $PE = 3$, $ER = 6$. Is $DE \parallel QR$?

**Solution:**

Check: $\dfrac{PD}{DQ} = \dfrac{4}{8} = \dfrac{1}{2}$ and $\dfrac{PE}{ER} = \dfrac{3}{6} = \dfrac{1}{2}$

Both ratios equal $\dfrac{1}{2}$. By the **converse of BPT**, $DE \parallel QR$. ✓

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="ex4" label="Midpoint case">

**Q:** $D$ and $E$ are midpoints of $AB$ and $AC$ in $\triangle ABC$. What is $DE$ in terms of $BC$?

**Solution:**

$D$ is midpoint → $AD = DB$ → $\dfrac{AD}{DB} = 1$

By BPT → $\dfrac{AE}{EC} = 1$ → $E$ is midpoint of $AC$ ✓ (consistent)

Using Form 2: $\dfrac{AD}{AB} = \dfrac{1}{2}$ → $\dfrac{DE}{BC} = \dfrac{1}{2}$ → $DE = \dfrac{BC}{2}$

**This is the Mid-Point Theorem:** The line joining midpoints of two sides = half the third side and is parallel to it.

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

---

## Exam writing format

```
In △ABC, DE ∥ BC (given)
By BPT: AD/DB = AE/EC
        4/6   = 5/EC
∴ EC = 30/4 = 7.5
```

Always state "By BPT" explicitly — that earns a method mark.

---

## Summary

:::tip BPT at a glance
**Statement:** $DE \parallel BC$ → $\dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}$

**Also gives:** $\dfrac{AD}{AB} = \dfrac{AE}{AC} = \dfrac{DE}{BC}$ (from the similarity $\triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC$)

**Converse:** $\dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}$ → $DE \parallel BC$

**Special case:** $D$, $E$ are midpoints → $DE = \dfrac{1}{2}BC$ (Mid-Point Theorem)
:::
