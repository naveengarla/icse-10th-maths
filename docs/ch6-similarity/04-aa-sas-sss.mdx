# 4. The Three Conditions — AA, SAS, SSS

## Why do we need conditions at all?

In the previous pages you learned that similar triangles have **equal angles** and **proportional sides**. But to check ALL of that for two triangles, you'd need to measure 3 angles and 3 sides — 6 measurements every time.

Mathematicians discovered shortcuts. In some cases, checking just **2 angles** or **1 angle + 2 sides** is enough to guarantee similarity. These shortcuts are the three conditions.

:::note The three conditions — overview
| Condition | What to check | How much work |
|-----------|--------------|--------------|
| **AA** | 2 pairs of equal angles | Easiest — most common |
| **SAS** | 1 equal angle + 2 proportional sides AROUND it | Medium |
| **SSS** | All 3 pairs of sides proportional | Most work |

**In 80% of ICSE questions: use AA.**
:::

---

## Condition 1 — AA (Angle-Angle)

### Start here: why 2 angles are enough

Before we look at any triangles, let's understand ONE fact that makes AA possible:

> **In any triangle, all three angles add up to 180°.**
> So if you know TWO angles, the third is completely determined — it has no choice.

<ThirdAngleForced />

**The implication:** If $\angle A = \angle P$ and $\angle B = \angle Q$ in two triangles, then mathematically:

$$\angle C = 180° - \angle A - \angle B = 180° - \angle P - \angle Q = \angle R$$

The third angle MUST match too. So checking just 2 is genuinely sufficient.

---

### The AA condition stated precisely

:::note AA Condition
If **two angles** of one triangle are equal to **two angles** of another triangle, the triangles are similar.

If $\angle A = \angle P$ and $\angle B = \angle Q$, then $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR$.
:::

<AACondition />

---

### Where do equal angles come from? (The 3 sources)

This is the part students often miss. In an exam figure, equal angles don't appear by magic — they come from one of three geometric facts. You need to recognise these on sight.

<AngleSources />

<Tabs>
  <TabItem value="common" label="Source 1 — Common Angle">

**A shared vertex gives a common angle.**

When two triangles share a vertex point, the angle at that vertex is **the same angle** in both triangles.

**Example:** $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle ADE$ both have vertex $A$ at the top.

$\angle DAE = \angle BAC$ — they are literally the SAME angle.

✅ **Write in proof:** "$\angle A$ is common to both triangles"

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="parallel" label="Source 2 — Parallel Lines">

**Parallel lines create equal corresponding angles.**

When a line $DE$ is parallel to $BC$, the transversal $AB$ cuts both lines creating **corresponding angles**.

$\angle ADE = \angle ABC$ (corresponding angles, $DE \parallel BC$)

Similarly: $\angle AED = \angle ACB$

✅ **Write in proof:** "$\angle ADE = \angle ABC$ (corresponding angles, $DE \parallel BC$)"

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="vertical" label="Source 3 — Vertically Opposite">

**Two lines crossing create equal vertically opposite angles.**

When diagonals of a quadrilateral cross at point $O$:

$\angle AOB = \angle COD$ (vertically opposite angles)

This is very common in trapezium problems where diagonals intersect.

✅ **Write in proof:** "$\angle AOB = \angle COD$ (vertically opposite angles)"

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

---

### AA — Step by step example (the most common ICSE type)

<AAStepByStep />

:::tip Exam format — write it exactly like this
```
In △ADE and △ABC:
  ∠DAE = ∠BAC    ... (common angle at A)
  ∠ADE = ∠ABC    ... (corresponding angles, DE ∥ BC)
∴ △ADE ~ △ABC    ... (AA)
```
Two reasons, two angles, one conclusion. That's 3 marks in ICSE.
:::

---

### AA — More examples with increasing difficulty

<Tabs>
  <TabItem value="aa1" label="Example 1 — Basic">

**Figure:** $\triangle ABC$ with $\angle A = 65°$, $\angle B = 50°$. Another $\triangle PQR$ with $\angle P = 65°$, $\angle Q = 50°$.

**Proof:**
```
In △ABC and △PQR:
  ∠A = ∠P = 65°   ... (given)
  ∠B = ∠Q = 50°   ... (given)
∴ △ABC ~ △PQR     ... (AA)
```

**Note:** We didn't need to check $\angle C$ and $\angle R$ — they're automatically both $180° - 65° - 50° = 65°$.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="aa2" label="Example 2 — Shared Angle">

**Figure:** In $\triangle ABC$, $D$ is on $AB$ and $E$ is on $AC$ such that $DE \parallel BC$.

**Prove $\triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC$.**

```
In △ADE and △ABC:
  ∠DAE = ∠BAC   ... (same angle — A is common vertex)
  ∠ADE = ∠ABC   ... (DE ∥ BC → corresponding angles)
∴ △ADE ~ △ABC   ... (AA)
```

**Careful with letter order:** A matches A (common), D matches B (both have angle β), E matches C.
So the correct statement is $\triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC$ — NOT $\triangle ADE \sim \triangle BAC$.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="aa3" label="Example 3 — Diagonals">

**Figure:** $ABCD$ is a trapezium with $AB \parallel CD$. Diagonals meet at $O$. Prove $\triangle AOB \sim \triangle COD$.

```
In △AOB and △COD:
  ∠AOB = ∠COD   ... (vertically opposite angles)
  ∠OAB = ∠OCD   ... (AB ∥ CD → alternate angles)
∴ △AOB ~ △COD   ... (AA)
```

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="aa4" label="Example 4 — Right Angles">

**Figure:** In $\triangle ABC$, $\angle BAC = 90°$. $AD \perp BC$ where $D$ is on $BC$. Prove $\triangle ABD \sim \triangle CAD$.

**Trick:** Both triangles have a 90° angle. Find ONE more matching angle.

```
In △ABD and △CAD:
  ∠ADB = ∠CDA = 90°   ... (AD ⊥ BC, given)
  ∠ABD = ∠CAD         ... (both = 90° − ∠BAD → complementary)
∴ △ABD ~ △CAD        ... (AA)
```

**Why $\angle ABD = \angle CAD$?** In $\triangle ABD$: $\angle ABD + \angle BAD = 90°$. In $\triangle CAD$: $\angle CAD + \angle ACD = 90°$. But $\angle ACD = \angle ABD$ (same angle from the original). So $\angle BAD = \angle ACD$... work through it carefully.

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

---

### AA — Edge cases and traps

<Tabs>
  <TabItem value="e1" label="❌ Only 1 angle equal">

**Wrong:** "∠A = ∠P (given). Therefore △ABC ~ △PQR by AA."

**Why wrong:** AA requires **two** pairs of equal angles. One is not enough — you could have the same angle but completely different other angles.

**Fix:** Find the second pair before concluding.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="e2" label="❌ Wrong letter order">

**Wrong:** $\angle A = \angle Q$ and $\angle B = \angle P$. Writing "$\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR$."

**Why wrong:** If A matches Q and B matches P, then the correct statement is $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle QPR$ (Q first, P second, R third).

**Rule:** The position of the letter in the similarity statement MUST match the vertex with the equal angle.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="e3" label="✅ When only 1 angle is given">

**Situation:** You know $\angle A = \angle P$ but no other angles are given.

**What to do:** Look for a reason why another pair might be equal:
1. Is there a common angle?
2. Are there parallel lines creating corresponding angles?
3. Are there right angles given?

If you find none, AA doesn't apply — try SAS or SSS.

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## Condition 2 — SAS (Side-Angle-Side)

### The word "included" explained carefully

SAS sounds simple but has one word that confuses everyone: **included**.

The equal angle must be **included** between the two proportional sides. This means: the angle must be at the vertex where those two sides meet.

<IncludedAngleDiagram />

**Think of it this way:** If you pick any vertex of a triangle, that vertex has exactly two sides coming out of it. The angle at that vertex is "included" by those two sides — it sits between them.

---

### SAS stated precisely

:::note SAS Condition
If **one angle** of a triangle equals one angle of another triangle, AND the **two sides FORMING that angle** are proportional, the triangles are similar.

If $\angle B = \angle Q$ AND $\dfrac{AB}{PQ} = \dfrac{BC}{QR}$, then $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR$.
:::

<SASCondition />

---

### How to apply SAS — step by step

**Step 1:** Identify the equal angles (which vertices match).

**Step 2:** Check the two sides AT each of those vertices (the included sides).

**Step 3:** Compute their ratios. Are they equal?

**Step 4:** If yes → SAS. Write the conclusion.

<Tabs>
  <TabItem value="sas1" label="Example 1 — Basic SAS">

**Given:** $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle PQR$ with $\angle B = \angle Q = 70°$, $AB = 4$, $PQ = 6$, $BC = 6$, $QR = 9$.

**Step 1:** Equal angle at B and Q. So B↔Q in the similarity.

**Step 2:** Sides at B: $AB$ and $BC$. Sides at Q: $PQ$ and $QR$.

**Step 3:** $\dfrac{PQ}{AB} = \dfrac{6}{4} = 1.5$ and $\dfrac{QR}{BC} = \dfrac{9}{6} = 1.5$. Equal ✓

**Proof:**
```
In △ABC and △PQR:
  ∠B = ∠Q = 70°         ... (given)
  AB/PQ = BC/QR = 3/2   ... (given: 4/6 = 6/9 = 2/3)
∴ △ABC ~ △PQR           ... (SAS)
```

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="sas2" label="Example 2 — Find Unknown">

**Given:** $\triangle DEF \sim \triangle XYZ$ by SAS. $\angle E = \angle Y$, $DE = 5$, $XY = 10$, $EF = 8$. Find $YZ$.

Since $\angle E = \angle Y$ and SAS applies:

$\dfrac{XY}{DE} = \dfrac{YZ}{EF}$

$\dfrac{10}{5} = \dfrac{YZ}{8}$

$YZ = 2 \times 8 = 16$

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="sas3" label="❌ SAS Trap — Wrong Angle">

**Wrong attempt:** $\angle A = \angle P$, $AB = 3$, $PQ = 6$, $BC = 4$, $QR = 8$. "SAS applies."

**Why wrong:** $\angle A$ is at vertex A, which is between sides $AB$ and $AC$. But $BC$ is the side opposite to $A$ — NOT adjacent to $A$.

For SAS at $\angle A$: you need sides $AB$ and $AC$ to be proportional (not $AB$ and $BC$).

**Moral:** The two proportional sides MUST both come out of the equal angle's vertex.

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## Condition 3 — SSS (Side-Side-Side)

### The idea

If ALL THREE pairs of corresponding sides are in the same ratio, the triangles must be similar.

:::note SSS Condition
If $\dfrac{AB}{PQ} = \dfrac{BC}{QR} = \dfrac{CA}{RP}$, then $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR$.
:::

<SSSCondition />

---

### How to apply SSS — step by step

**The challenge with SSS:** You need to know which sides correspond. Unlike AA, where angles tell you the matching, SSS requires you to figure out the matching from the side ratios themselves.

**Method:** Sort all sides from smallest to largest in each triangle. Match smallest↔smallest, middle↔middle, largest↔largest. Then check all three ratios.

<Tabs>
  <TabItem value="sss1" label="Example 1 — Basic">

$\triangle ABC$: sides 3, 4, 5. $\triangle PQR$: sides 6, 8, 10.

**Sort and match:**

| △ABC | △PQR | Ratio |
|------|------|-------|
| 3 (BC, smallest) | 6 (QR, smallest) | 6/3 = **2** |
| 4 (AB, middle)   | 8 (PQ, middle)   | 8/4 = **2** |
| 5 (CA, largest)  | 10 (RP, largest) | 10/5 = **2** |

All ratios = 2. $\therefore \triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR$ **(SSS)** with $k = 2$.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="sss2" label="Example 2 — NOT similar">

$\triangle ABC$: sides 4, 6, 8. $\triangle PQR$: sides 6, 9, 13.

| △ABC | △PQR | Ratio |
|------|------|-------|
| 4 | 6 | 6/4 = **1.5** |
| 6 | 9 | 9/6 = **1.5** |
| 8 | 13 | 13/8 = **1.625** |

Third ratio differs. **Not similar.** Even one unequal ratio breaks SSS.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="sss3" label="❌ SSS Trap — Wrong pairing">

**Wrong:** $\triangle ABC$ has sides 3, 5, 7. $\triangle PQR$ has sides 6, 10, 14.

Student checks: $6/3=2$, $14/5=2.8$ ← ❌

**Why wrong:** They matched 14 (largest of PQR) with 5 (middle of ABC). Always pair same rank sides.

**Correct:** 6/3=2, 10/5=2, 14/7=2. Similar! ✓

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## Practice — identify the correct condition

<Tabs>
  <TabItem value="p1" label="Practice 1">

**Figure:** $\triangle LMN$ with $LM = 4$, $MN = 6$, $\angle M = 50°$. $\triangle XYZ$ with $XY = 8$, $YZ = 12$, $\angle Y = 50°$.

Which condition? What is the similarity statement?

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="a1" label="Answer 1">

$\angle M = \angle Y = 50°$ (equal included angle).

Check sides at M: LM and MN. At Y: XY and YZ.

$\dfrac{XY}{LM} = \dfrac{8}{4} = 2$ and $\dfrac{YZ}{MN} = \dfrac{12}{6} = 2$ ✓

**SAS:** $\triangle LMN \sim \triangle XYZ$ with $k = 2$.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="p2" label="Practice 2">

**Figure:** In $\triangle PQR$, $S$ is a point on $QR$ such that $PS \perp QR$. Given $\angle QPR = 90°$.

Prove $\triangle PQS \sim \triangle RPS$.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="a2" label="Answer 2">

```
In △PQS and △RPS:
  ∠PSQ = ∠PSR = 90°   ... (PS ⊥ QR, given)
  ∠PQS = ∠RPS         ... (both = 90° − ∠QPS, since ∠QPR = 90°)
∴ △PQS ~ △RPS        ... (AA)
```

Note: $\angle QPS + \angle PQS = 90°$ (in $\triangle PQS$). Also $\angle RPS + \angle PRS = 90°$ (in $\triangle RPS$). But $\angle PRS = \angle PQS$... wait, actually: $\angle QPS + \angle SPR = 90°$ (since $\angle QPR = 90°$). So $\angle PQS = 90° - \angle QPS$ and $\angle RPS = 90° - \angle QPS$. Equal! ✓

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="p3" label="Practice 3">

**Figure:** Two triangles with sides: $\triangle ABC$: 5, 7, 9. $\triangle PQR$: 10, 14, 18.

Are they similar? State the condition and scale factor.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="a3" label="Answer 3">

Check ratios (sort both sets): 5/10? No — match large to large: 10/5=2, 14/7=2, 18/9=2. All equal.

**SSS:** $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR$ with $k = 2$.

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## Summary

:::tip The complete picture
| | AA | SAS | SSS |
|--|----|----|-----|
| **Check** | 2 angle pairs | 1 angle + 2 adjacent side ratios | 3 side ratios |
| **Key word** | Common / corresponding / vertically opposite | **Included** angle | All three **equal ratios** |
| **Exam frequency** | 🔥 Most common | Medium | Less common |
| **Easiest mistake** | Wrong letter order | Angle not included | Wrong side pairing |

**For every proof:** state the equal angles/sides, give a reason for each, then write the conclusion with the condition name.
:::
