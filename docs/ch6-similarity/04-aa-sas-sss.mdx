# 4. The Three Conditions — AA, SAS, SSS

## The central question of this chapter

Given two triangles — how do you PROVE they are similar without measuring every angle and every side?

Mathematicians discovered that you don't need to check everything. Just three shortcuts work.

:::note The three conditions
If any ONE of these is true, the triangles are similar:
1. **AA** — Two pairs of angles are equal
2. **SAS** — One pair of angles is equal AND the sides around that angle are proportional
3. **SSS** — All three pairs of corresponding sides are proportional
:::

---

## Condition 1 — AA (Angle-Angle)

### The idea

If two angles of one triangle equal two angles of another triangle — the triangles are similar.

**Why only two angles?** Because angles in a triangle always add up to 180°. So if two angles match, the third one is forced to match too (180° minus the other two).

$$\angle A = \angle P \text{ and } \angle B = \angle Q \implies \angle C = \angle R \text{ automatically}$$

### Visualised

<AACondition />

### When to use AA

AA is the **most commonly used** condition in ICSE problems because:
- You only need to spot 2 equal angles
- Parallel lines automatically create equal corresponding angles (very common in figures)
- A shared angle counts as a "free" equal angle

### AA in practice

<Tabs>
  <TabItem value="parallel" label="Using Parallel Lines">

**Figure:** In $\triangle ABC$, $DE \parallel BC$ where $D$ is on $AB$ and $E$ is on $AC$.

**Why is $\triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC$?**

- $\angle A = \angle A$ — **common angle** (shared by both triangles)
- $\angle ADE = \angle ABC$ — **corresponding angles** (since $DE \parallel BC$)

Two angles match → **AA** → $\triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC$

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="shared" label="Using a Shared Angle">

**Figure:** In $\triangle ABC$, $\angle ADB = 90°$ where $D$ is the foot of altitude from $A$.

**Why is $\triangle ABD \sim \triangle ACD$?**

- $\angle ADB = \angle ADC = 90°$
- $\angle B = \angle B$ in $\triangle ABD$, $\angle A = \angle A$ in $\triangle ACD$...

Actually: $\angle ABD$ in $\triangle ABD$ and $\angle DAC$ in $\triangle ACD$ — these are complementary pairs. A shared vertex creates common angles.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="vertical" label="Vertically Opposite Angles">

**Figure:** Diagonals of a quadrilateral intersect at $O$.

$\angle AOB = \angle COD$ — **vertically opposite angles** (equal)

If also $\angle OAB = \angle OCD$ — **alternate angles** (if $AB \parallel CD$)

Then $\triangle AOB \sim \triangle COD$ by **AA**.

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## Condition 2 — SAS (Side-Angle-Side)

### The idea

One angle of the first triangle equals one angle of the second triangle, AND the two sides that form that angle are in the same ratio in both triangles.

$$\angle A = \angle P \quad \text{and} \quad \dfrac{AB}{PQ} = \dfrac{AC}{PR}$$

Then $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR$.

### The critical word: INCLUDED

The equal angle must be the angle **between** (included by) the two proportional sides. An angle at one corner, with the two sides of that corner being the proportional ones.

<SASCondition />

### SAS in practice

<Tabs>
  <TabItem value="example" label="Worked Example">

In $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle PQR$:
- $\angle B = \angle Q = 60°$
- $AB = 4$, $PQ = 6$
- $BC = 6$, $QR = 9$

**Check:** $\dfrac{PQ}{AB} = \dfrac{6}{4} = 1.5$ and $\dfrac{QR}{BC} = \dfrac{9}{6} = 1.5$ ✓

Equal ratio + included angle $\angle B = \angle Q$

**Conclusion:** $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR$ by **SAS** with $k = 1.5$

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="trap" label="⚠️ The SAS Trap">

**What goes wrong:** Students use SAS when the equal angle is NOT between the two proportional sides.

**Example of wrong usage:**

$\angle A = \angle P$ (at vertex A and P), sides $BC = 5$ and $QR = 10$ (ratio 2), sides $AB = 3$ and $PQ = 6$ (ratio 2).

But wait — $\angle A$ is the angle at vertex $A$, which is between sides $AB$ and $AC$. The side $BC$ is NOT adjacent to $\angle A$.

So $BC/QR$ cannot be used for SAS with angle $A$.

**Rule:** Only use sides that meet at the equal angle.

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## Condition 3 — SSS (Side-Side-Side)

### The idea

All three pairs of corresponding sides are in the same ratio.

$$\dfrac{AB}{PQ} = \dfrac{BC}{QR} = \dfrac{CA}{RP} = k$$

If all three ratios are equal → the triangles are similar.

<SSSCondition />

### SSS in practice

<Tabs>
  <TabItem value="check" label="How to Check SSS">

**Step 1:** List the sides of both triangles in order (smallest to largest or matched by position).

**Step 2:** Compute each ratio: $\dfrac{\text{new}}{\text{original}}$.

**Step 3:** If all three ratios are identical → SSS → Similar.

**Example:**

$\triangle ABC$: sides 5, 7, 9. $\triangle PQR$: sides 10, 14, 18.

$10/5 = 2$, $14/7 = 2$, $18/9 = 2$. All equal → **SSS** → $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR$ with $k = 2$.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="not-similar" label="When SSS Fails">

**Example:**

$\triangle ABC$: sides 3, 4, 5. $\triangle PQR$: sides 6, 9, 10.

$6/3 = 2$, $9/4 = 2.25$, $10/5 = 2$. 

The middle ratio is different. **NOT similar.**

Even one unequal ratio breaks similarity.

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## Comparing all three conditions

| Condition | What you check | Most common use |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| **AA** | 2 pairs of equal angles | Parallel lines, shared angles, alternate angles |
| **SAS** | 1 equal angle + the 2 sides around it are proportional | When angles are given with some sides |
| **SSS** | All 3 side ratios equal | When all sides are given, no angles |

:::tip Which condition to use in an exam?
- **Parallel lines in the figure?** → Almost always **AA**
- **One angle given, some sides given?** → Try **SAS**
- **All sides given, no angles?** → **SSS**
- **Two angles mentioned or provable?** → **AA** (easiest)
:::

---

## Writing the proof — exam format

ICSE expects you to state the condition clearly. Here is the exact format:

```
In △ABC and △PQR:
  ∠A = ∠P    ... (given / alternate angles / corresponding angles)
  ∠B = ∠Q    ... (give reason)
∴ △ABC ~ △PQR    ... (AA)
```

Always:
1. Write the two triangles being compared
2. State the equal angles / proportional sides
3. Give a reason for each
4. State the conclusion with the condition used

## Quick practice

<Tabs>
  <TabItem value="q1" label="Q1">

In $\triangle ABC$, $DE \parallel BC$ ($D$ on $AB$, $E$ on $AC$). Write a similarity statement and state the condition.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="a1" label="Answer 1">

In $\triangle ADE$ and $\triangle ABC$:

- $\angle DAE = \angle BAC$ (common angle at $A$)
- $\angle ADE = \angle ABC$ (corresponding angles, $DE \parallel BC$)

$\therefore \triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC$ (AA)

**Note the order:** $A$ matches $A$, $D$ matches $B$, $E$ matches $C$.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="q2" label="Q2">

$\triangle PQR$ has $PQ = 6$, $QR = 8$, $PR = 10$. $\triangle XYZ$ has $XY = 3$, $YZ = 4$, $XZ = 5$.

Are they similar? State the condition.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="a2" label="Answer 2">

$\dfrac{PQ}{XY} = \dfrac{6}{3} = 2$, $\quad \dfrac{QR}{YZ} = \dfrac{8}{4} = 2$, $\quad \dfrac{PR}{XZ} = \dfrac{10}{5} = 2$

All ratios equal 2. $\therefore \triangle PQR \sim \triangle XYZ$ **(SSS)** with $k = 2$.

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## Summary

:::tip Remember
- **AA** — 2 angles equal (third is automatic) — most common
- **SAS** — 1 angle equal AND the sides AROUND that angle are proportional
- **SSS** — all 3 side ratios equal
- In ICSE: AA appears in 80% of questions — master it first
:::
